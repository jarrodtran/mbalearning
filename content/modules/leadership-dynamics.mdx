# MODULE 4:INTERPERSONAL DYNAMICS & LEADERSHIP
## Inspired by Stanford Graduate School of Business

### Executive Briefing (SCQA)

**Situation:** Technical skills and strategic frameworks get you to the table. Leadership is what determines what happens once you're there. The most analytically gifted executives often plateau not because their thinking is wrong but because they cannot get organizations to move, cannot navigate conflict productively, and cannot communicate in ways that inspire rather than merely inform.

**Complication:** Leadership is the most studied and least understood subject in business education. Thousands of books, hundreds of frameworks, decades of research — and most organizations still struggle with fundamental problems of culture, motivation, and execution. The Stanford GSB approach starts from a different premise: leadership development begins with self-knowledge, not technique.

**Question:** How do executives develop the self-awareness, emotional intelligence, and communication capabilities required to lead effectively across different contexts — managing up, managing peers, building teams, navigating conflict, and making decisions under pressure?

**Answer:** By developing a rigorous understanding of their own leadership style and its shadow side, mastering the specific skills of executive communication, building frameworks for delegation and decision rights, and learning to navigate the interpersonal dynamics of power, conflict, and motivation that determine organizational outcomes.

### Core Framework 1: Leadership Styles and Their Contexts

Daniel Goleman's research (published in Harvard Business Review) identified six distinct leadership styles. The critical insight is that no single style is universally effective. The most effective leaders are situationally fluent — they can read the context and deploy the appropriate style.

#### Style 1: Visionary (Authoritative)
**The approach:** "Come with me." The leader articulates a compelling vision of the future and gives people the freedom to find their own way toward it.

**When it works:** When a team or organization needs a new direction. When the leader has genuine credibility and the vision is genuinely compelling. When team members are competent and need direction, not instruction.

**When it fails:** When the leader's vision is wrong or incomplete. When execution requires detailed coordination that "come with me" doesn't provide. When the team needs more development before they can self-direct.

**The shadow side:** Visionary leaders can become blind to disconfirming evidence. Their certainty — which inspires — can also prevent them from updating their model when facts change.

#### Style 2: Coaching
**The approach:** "Try this." The leader focuses on people's long-term development, tolerating short-term failure as an investment in future capability.

**When it works:** When team members want to develop and are open to feedback. When there is time for development. When the leader has genuine insight into what the person needs to improve.

**When it fails:** In genuine crises where results matter more than development. When the team member lacks the motivation to develop. When the leader uses coaching as a way to avoid making hard decisions.

**The shadow side:** Coaching can become avoidance. "Let me help you figure this out" can be a way of not making the call yourself.

#### Style 3: Affiliative
**The approach:** "People come first." The leader prioritizes emotional connection, team harmony, and morale.

**When it works:** When a team is fractured and needs healing. When individuals are going through personal difficulty that's affecting performance. When building connection across a new team.

**When it fails:** When performance issues are real and need to be addressed. When the need for harmony suppresses the honest feedback that would help people improve. When the leader avoids conflict so consistently that performance problems become structural.

**The shadow side:** Affiliative leaders often cannot deliver difficult feedback. Their teams may like them but not respect them in the ways that produce high performance.

#### Style 4: Democratic
**The approach:** "What do you think?" The leader builds consensus through participation, gathering input before making decisions.

**When it works:** When the leader genuinely doesn't have the answer and the team does. When buy-in is essential for execution. When there is time to deliberate.

**When it fails:** In genuine crises or time-compressed decisions. When the leader uses democracy as a way of avoiding accountability ("I just did what the group decided"). When team members want direction, not participation.

**The shadow side:** Democratic leadership at its worst produces slow decisions, mediocre consensus outcomes, and the diffusion of accountability.

#### Style 5: Pacesetting
**The approach:** "Do as I do, now." The leader sets extremely high performance standards and exemplifies them personally. Poor performers are expected to improve or leave.

**When it works:** With highly motivated, highly competent teams that need little direction. In technical domains where the leader has superior knowledge. For short periods of intense execution.

**When it fails:** Over extended periods, this style creates anxiety, burnout, and departure of everyone who doesn't meet the leader's standard — including people who might have developed into high performers with more support. It also creates cultures that are good at execution but terrible at innovation (people execute but never experiment).

**The shadow side:** Pacesetting leaders often don't know how to coach. They can do it, but they can't explain it. Their teams develop dependence rather than capability.

#### Style 6: Commanding (Coercive)
**The approach:** "Do what I tell you." The leader demands immediate compliance and closely monitors performance.

**When it works:** In genuine crises (a burning building, a company in freefall). When dealing with a genuinely problematic employee who is not responding to other approaches.

**When it fails:** In essentially every other context. Research consistently shows this is the worst style for long-term organizational performance. It destroys morale, psychological safety, and intrinsic motivation.

**The shadow side:** Commanding leaders often produce results in the short term — which validates the style — while creating organizational damage (talent departure, psychological unsafety, reduced initiative) that isn't visible until much later.

### Core Framework 2: Emotional Intelligence

Daniel Goleman's emotional intelligence (EI) framework identifies five components that distinguish effective leaders from technically competent managers who don't lead well:

#### Self-Awareness
The ability to recognize and understand your own emotions, strengths, weaknesses, values, and motivations — and how they affect others.

Self-awareness is the foundation. You cannot manage others effectively if you cannot observe your own reactions, identify your triggers, and understand how your mood and behavior affect the people around you.

**Practical indicator:** Can you articulate your own blind spots? Do you know the conditions under which you become less effective? Do you have a clear sense of how you come across in high-pressure situations?

#### Self-Regulation
The ability to control or redirect your disruptive emotions and impulses, and to think before acting.

Self-regulation does not mean suppressing emotion. It means not letting emotions drive behavior in ways that undermine your effectiveness or damage relationships. The executive who erupts in frustration when things go wrong, or who withdraws when challenged, is operating from a deficit of self-regulation.

**Practical indicator:** When you receive unexpected bad news in a meeting, what is your default response? How long does it take you to regain equilibrium after a setback?

#### Motivation
A passion for work that goes beyond money and status — an intrinsic drive to achieve, a commitment to improvement, and an optimism that persists even in the face of setbacks.

Motivated leaders set high standards (for themselves and their teams), seek continuous improvement, and maintain energy and optimism during adversity. This energy is contagious — teams led by genuinely motivated leaders tend to develop the same orientation.

**Practical indicator:** What gets you out of bed? Can you articulate why the work matters to you beyond compensation and career advancement?

#### Empathy
The ability to understand and share the feelings of others — to process the emotional content of a situation alongside its logical content.

Empathy is not softness. It is an information system. Leaders with high empathy understand what their teams are experiencing, anticipate how decisions will land, and can communicate in ways that connect. Leaders with low empathy are often surprised by human responses to their decisions — "I didn't think they would react that way" is often a symptom of low empathy.

**Practical indicator:** After making a major decision, can you accurately predict how different team members will react — both the logical reaction ("this doesn't make strategic sense") and the emotional reaction ("this makes me feel devalued")?

#### Social Skill
The ability to manage relationships and build networks — to find common ground, build rapport, and move people in the direction you want to go.

Social skill is not about charm or manipulation. It is about being good at building relationships across a range of people, finding genuine common ground, and persuading rather than demanding.

### Core Framework 3: Decision Rights and Delegation

One of the most common organizational failures is ambiguity about who has the right to make which decisions. The RACI matrix is the standard tool for clarifying decision rights:

**R — Responsible:** Who actually does the work to make the decision happen?
**A — Accountable:** Who has final decision authority and is answerable for the outcome? (Only one person per decision)
**C — Consulted:** Whose input is sought before the decision is made?
**I — Informed:** Who needs to know the decision after it is made?

The most common RACI failures:
- Multiple people marked as Accountable (shared accountability = no accountability)
- No one marked as Accountable (the decision disappears)
- Everyone marked as Consulted (every decision requires consensus, nothing moves)
- Consulted people treated as Accountable (they feel ownership but have no authority)

#### The Delegation Matrix

Not everything should be delegated equally. The appropriate level of delegation depends on two factors:
1. **The importance of the decision** — What is the cost of a wrong decision?
2. **The capability of the team member** — How confident are you in their judgment?

This creates a 2x2 matrix:
- **High importance, high capability:** Delegate fully. Check in after, not before.
- **High importance, low capability:** Involve directly. Coach through the decision.
- **Low importance, high capability:** Delegate without follow-up. Trust completely.
- **Low importance, low capability:** Delegate with a clear framework. Use as a development opportunity.

### Core Framework 4: Navigating Conflict

Conflict is not a sign that something is wrong with your organization. It is a sign that people are engaged. The question is not how to avoid conflict but how to make conflict productive.

#### Conflict Management Styles (Thomas-Kilmann Model)

**Competing:** High assertiveness, low cooperation. Pursuing your own concerns at the other party's expense. Appropriate for crises, value clarification moments, or when you know you're right and the stakes are high.

**Collaborating:** High assertiveness, high cooperation. Working with the other party to find a solution that fully satisfies both. Appropriate for important conflicts where both parties' concerns are too important to compromise.

**Compromising:** Moderate assertiveness, moderate cooperation. Finding an expedient, mutually acceptable solution that partially satisfies both parties. Appropriate for moderately important issues, when time is short, when collaboration has failed.

**Avoiding:** Low assertiveness, low cooperation. Not pursuing your own concerns or the other party's. Appropriate for trivial issues, when you need time to cool down, when the potential damage of confrontation exceeds the benefit of resolution.

**Accommodating:** Low assertiveness, high cooperation. Neglecting your own concerns to satisfy the other party. Appropriate when the issue is more important to the other person, when you realize you're wrong, or as a gesture of good faith.

**The executive trap:** Most executives are systematically better at some styles than others. Leaders with high dominance instincts default to competing even when collaboration would produce better outcomes. Affiliative leaders default to accommodating even when they should compete. Self-awareness is the precondition for situational fluency.

### Case Study: Satya Nadella's Leadership Transformation at Microsoft

#### The Situation

When Satya Nadella became CEO of Microsoft in February 2014, the company was widely regarded as a failing giant. Market capitalization had been essentially flat for over a decade. The culture, under Steve Ballmer's leadership, was characterized by stack ranking — a performance management system that forced every team to rank employees on a bell curve, with a mandatory percentage of poor ratings regardless of team performance. The result was a culture of internal competition, political maneuvering, and risk avoidance. People competed against colleagues rather than collaborating with them.

Microsoft had missed the mobile revolution (Windows Phone was a catastrophic failure). It had missed the search revolution (Bing was a distant second to Google). It had missed the cloud revolution's early wave (Amazon Web Services had a multi-year head start on Azure). And it had a cultural identity crisis — it was a company that defined itself by its products (Windows, Office) in an era when those products were becoming less relevant.

#### Nadella's Leadership Intervention

**First move — Change the mental model:** Nadella replaced "know-it-all" culture with "learn-it-all" culture. Explicitly. He drew on Carol Dweck's growth mindset research and made it the cultural foundation of his leadership. In a company where the smartest person in the room was always trying to prove they were the smartest person in the room, this was radical.

**Second move — Change the strategy:** Nadella articulated a clear vision — "Mobile-first, cloud-first" — that acknowledged Microsoft's failures without dwelling on them and set a direction that the organization could rally around. The vision was specific enough to drive decisions but broad enough to accommodate diverse initiatives.

**Third move — Change the incentive structures:** Stack ranking was eliminated. Cross-functional collaboration was rewarded. The performance management system was restructured around growth and development rather than relative ranking.

**Fourth move — Model the behavior:** Nadella explicitly shared his own learning journey, his own mistakes, and his own development areas. In a culture where vulnerability had been weakness, this was a significant signal.

#### The Results

Microsoft's market capitalization went from approximately $300 billion when Nadella took over to over $2 trillion a decade later. Azure became the second-largest cloud platform in the world. Microsoft 365 transformed Office from a product into a subscription business. The acquisition of LinkedIn, GitHub, Activision, and dozens of other companies expanded the platform. Employee satisfaction scores improved significantly.

#### The Leadership Lesson

Nadella's transformation of Microsoft is a case study in the relationship between leadership style and organizational outcome. He did not change what Microsoft made. He changed how Microsoft thought about itself. The cultural transformation — from a fixed mindset company to a growth mindset company — was the precondition for all the strategic changes that followed. Leadership creates the conditions for strategy. Strategy without the right leadership environment cannot execute.
