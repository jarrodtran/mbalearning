# MODULE 1:THE EXECUTIVE CASE STUDY METHOD
## Inspired by Harvard Business School

### Executive Briefing (SCQA)

**Situation:** Every major business decision is made under conditions of incomplete information, competing stakeholder interests, time pressure, and genuine uncertainty about the future. This is not a bug in the system — it is the system. The question is not how to eliminate uncertainty, but how to make high-quality decisions despite it.

**Complication:** Most people approach business problems the wrong way. They either gather data until they feel confident (analysis paralysis) or they lead with a conclusion and work backward to justify it (confirmation bias). Neither produces good decisions. The first is too slow. The second is intellectually dishonest. Both fail under real boardroom pressure.

**Question:** How do the world's best business leaders structure their thinking so that they can synthesize ambiguous information, identify what actually matters, communicate a defensible position, and act decisively — all under pressure?

**Answer:** They use a disciplined case methodology. They separate facts from assumptions, map stakeholders against their interests, generate genuine strategic options (not a false menu), apply the right analytical frameworks, and communicate conclusions using a structured logic that works top-down — conclusion first, supporting arguments second, evidence third.

### Core Framework: The HBS Case Method

The case method is not about finding the right answer. It is about developing the judgment to identify the right question, and the discipline to work through it rigorously. HBS has used this approach since 1924. Here is the full architecture:

#### Step 1: Situation Analysis — Read the Room Before You Read the Data

Before touching a framework, an executive asks four questions:

1. **What is actually happening here?** — Distinguish observable facts from interpretations. Facts are what happened. Interpretations are what you think it means.
2. **What is the central tension?** — Every good case has one. It is usually between two legitimate values: growth vs. stability, short-term vs. long-term, stakeholder A vs. stakeholder B.
3. **What decision needs to be made?** — Be precise. "What should we do about our strategy?" is not a decision. "Should we acquire Netflix for $500M or build a competing service in-house?" is a decision.
4. **Who owns this decision?** — Authority, accountability, and information are rarely held by the same person. Map the gap.

#### Step 2: Stakeholder Mapping — Follow the Incentives

Every business situation involves people whose interests are partially aligned and partially in conflict. The naive analyst ignores this. The executive maps it explicitly.

For each stakeholder, identify:
- **Primary interest** — What do they most want from this situation?
- **Secondary interest** — What would they settle for?
- **Walk-away condition** — What outcome would cause them to defect, resign, or retaliate?
- **Influence** — How much power do they have to determine the outcome?
- **Information** — What do they know that others don't?

The stakeholder map is not just a list. It is a dynamic system. When you move one piece, others react. The executive thinks several moves ahead.

#### Step 3: Option Generation — Never Decide Between Two Things

One of the most common executive errors is presenting a binary choice: "We can do A or B." Binary choices are almost always artificial. The real strategic space has at least four options:

1. **The aggressive option** — Maximum resource commitment, maximum upside, maximum risk
2. **The defensive option** — Protect the core, minimize exposure, sacrifice upside
3. **The creative option** — A path that reframes the problem entirely
4. **The do-nothing option** — Explicit inaction, with a clear articulation of what you're betting on

Good option generation requires you to steelman each option — to make the strongest possible case for it before evaluating it.

#### Step 4: Analysis — Frameworks as Lenses, Not Answers

A framework is a structured way of seeing. It does not give you the answer. It tells you what questions to ask and in what order. The case method uses frameworks as diagnostic lenses:

- Use **Five Forces** to understand the industry's structural attractiveness
- Use **the Value Chain** to find where competitive advantage actually lives
- Use **the BCG Matrix** to allocate resources across a portfolio
- Use **the 3C framework** (Company, Customer, Competitor) as a quick situation scan
- Use **financial ratios** to understand the operational reality behind the income statement

The mistake is mistaking the framework for the answer. Frameworks are not conclusions. They are the beginning of the conversation.

#### Step 5: Recommendation — The Minto Pyramid Principle

Barbara Minto, a McKinsey consultant, developed the most important communication framework in business. The pyramid works as follows:

**Level 1 — The Governing Thought (Lead with your conclusion)**
State your recommendation in one sentence. Not "I think we should consider..." but "We should acquire Netflix at $500M because it is the only move that closes the digital distribution gap before streaming makes our physical network obsolete."

**Level 2 — The Three Supporting Arguments (MECE)**
Your governing thought must be supported by arguments that are:
- **Mutually Exclusive** — They don't repeat each other
- **Collectively Exhaustive** — Together, they fully prove your case

**Level 3 — The Evidence**
Each argument is supported by data, precedent, or logic. This is where your analysis lives — but it is at the bottom of the pyramid, not the top.

This structure works for emails, board presentations, investor memos, and conversations. It forces clarity of thought because you cannot structure your communication this way unless your thinking is already clear.

### Case Study: Blockbuster vs. Netflix (2000–2010)

#### Company Background

Blockbuster Inc. was founded in 1985 by David Cook in Dallas, Texas. By 2004, it was the world's largest video rental chain, operating approximately 9,000 stores globally and employing 60,000 people. Revenue exceeded $6 billion annually. Late fees — a deeply unpopular but economically critical revenue line — contributed approximately $800 million per year, roughly 14% of total revenue.

Netflix was founded in 1997 by Reed Hastings and Marc Randolph in Scotts Valley, California. The company began as a DVD-by-mail service, charging a flat monthly subscription with no late fees and no due dates. By 2000, Netflix was losing money and approached Blockbuster about an acquisition. Blockbuster passed. The asking price was $50 million.

By 2004, Netflix had crossed 2 million subscribers, growing at 74% year-over-year. Blockbuster's same-store revenue was declining at 8% annually.

#### The Central Dilemma

Jim Keyes, Blockbuster's CEO, faced a decision that would define the company's fate: How seriously should Blockbuster treat the Netflix threat, and what — if anything — should it do about it?

The complication was structural. Blockbuster's physical network was both its greatest asset and its greatest liability. 9,000 stores represented enormous fixed costs. The late fee revenue was economically essential but deeply unpopular. Any strategy that competed directly with Netflix required cannibalizing Blockbuster's own revenue streams — a politically and financially difficult move that the board, the franchisees, and the CFO would all resist.

#### Stakeholder Map

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Key Tension |
|---|---|---|
| Shareholders | Protect and grow $6B revenue base | Short-term EPS vs. long-term positioning |
| Franchisees (store operators) | Preserve store-level economics | Late fee elimination destroys their margin |
| Studio partners (Disney, Warner) | Maximum distribution reach | Would prefer Blockbuster healthy |
| Customers | Convenience, zero friction | Actively defecting to Netflix |
| Employees (60,000) | Job security | Livelihoods tied to physical stores |
| Board of Directors | Fiduciary duty to shareholders | Divided on severity of threat |
| Viacom (then-parent company) | Strategic coherence | Spun Blockbuster off in 2004 |

#### The Four Strategic Options

**Option 1: Defend the Core**
Double down on the physical store network. Invest in customer experience, expand merchandise categories, negotiate exclusive studio windows to differentiate content availability. Treat Netflix as a niche player serving a convenience-insensitive customer segment.

*Strongest argument for:* The physical network creates real switching costs. Studios need Blockbuster's distribution reach. Netflix is subscale and currently unprofitable.

*Fatal weakness:* This option assumes the competitive threat is static. Netflix is growing 74% per year. The window for a decisive response closes quickly.

**Option 2: Acquire Netflix**
Approach Netflix about acquisition. The 2004 market capitalization was approximately $500 million — ten times the 2000 asking price, but still potentially executable. Integration would give Blockbuster immediate digital capability and subscriber momentum.

*Strongest argument for:* Buy the threat. Gain the technology, the algorithm, the subscriber base, and the management team. Amazon did exactly this with Zappos and Whole Foods.

*Fatal weakness:* Integration risk is enormous. The cultures are opposed. Blockbuster's franchisees would resist. And the acqui-hire does nothing about the structural shift toward streaming.

**Option 3: Launch a Hybrid Attack**
Eliminate late fees systemically (even at the cost of $400M in annual revenue). Launch Blockbuster Online to compete directly with Netflix. Use the store network as a return/pickup advantage that Netflix physically cannot replicate — a genuine differentiation strategy.

*Strongest argument for:* This is the only option that leverages Blockbuster's unique asset (physical presence) while addressing the customer's primary complaint (late fees). The store-as-return-point is a sustainable differentiator.

*Fatal weakness:* Requires the board to accept a $400M revenue hole in year one. Politically nearly impossible. Also requires flawless execution of a new digital product while managing store operations.

**Option 4: Accelerate into Digital**
Recognize that the real threat is not Netflix's mail model but the coming shift to streaming (YouTube launched in beta in 2005). Invest aggressively in digital infrastructure — content licensing, streaming technology, and bandwidth — even at the cost of near-term profitability.

*Strongest argument for:* First-mover advantage in streaming could create durable competitive position. This is the only option that addresses the structural end-state rather than the current competitive threat.

*Fatal weakness:* Streaming infrastructure is expensive and the technology is nascent. This option requires the board to bet the company on a technology that hasn't yet proven mass market viability. It also abandons the core business before the transition is complete.

#### What Actually Happened

Blockbuster attempted Option 3 — too late and with insufficient conviction. CEO John Antioco launched Blockbuster Online in 2004 and eliminated late fees in 2005. The initiatives were strategically correct. But Carl Icahn, a major shareholder, viewed the late fee elimination as an expensive mistake and led a campaign to remove Antioco. Jim Keyes replaced him in 2007 and famously stated he didn't see Netflix as a threat. Keyes reversed the online strategy, reinvested in physical stores, and eliminated the digital initiatives.

Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy in 2010. Netflix's market capitalization today exceeds $250 billion.

#### Three Timeless Lessons

**Lesson 1: Innovator's Dilemma is structural, not psychological.**
Blockbuster's failure was not caused by stupidity. Several of their executives correctly identified the threat and the appropriate response. The failure was structural: the people who owned the decision (board, franchisees, activist shareholders) had financial interests tied to the existing model. The system selected against the correct strategy. This happens in every industry. The lesson is not "be smarter" — it is "change the decision-making structure before you need to make the hard call."

**Lesson 2: Competitive advantage can become a competitive trap.**
Blockbuster's physical network was a genuine competitive advantage for fifteen years. It created barriers to entry, generated customer relationships, and produced cash flow. Then it became a liability — a fixed cost structure that made the company slow to respond and financially fragile. The lesson is to periodically ask: "Is this asset still an asset in the world that is coming, not the world that exists today?"

**Lesson 3: Revenue is not the same as value.**
The $800M in late fee revenue felt essential because it was. But it was revenue extracted from customer dissatisfaction. The moment a competitor offered the same product without the penalty, customers left. True competitive advantage creates value for customers. Revenue that comes from customer pain is a liability disguised as an asset.

### The SCQA Framework — Applied

The SCQA framework (Situation, Complication, Question, Answer) is the foundation of executive communication. Every strategy memo, board presentation, and investor letter should open with this structure.

**Situation:** The background context that the audience already knows and agrees with. This is not your argument — it is the shared starting point.

**Complication:** The thing that happened (or is happening) that makes the situation problematic. This is the reason you are having the conversation at all. It creates the tension.

**Question:** The question that the complication naturally raises. Often implicit. Sometimes the most powerful move is to make it explicit.

**Answer:** Your recommendation. Stated directly. This is where most people are too timid — they hedge, they qualify, they offer options. The executive states a position.

**Example (Blockbuster, 2004):**
- *Situation:* Blockbuster is the category leader in home video with $6B in revenue and 9,000 stores globally.
- *Complication:* Netflix has grown 74% year-over-year to 2 million subscribers by offering a structurally superior model — subscription, no late fees, home delivery — that directly attacks Blockbuster's most unpopular customer experience elements.
- *Question:* How aggressively should Blockbuster respond, and what form should that response take?
- *Answer:* Blockbuster should immediately launch a hybrid attack — eliminate late fees, accelerate Blockbuster Online, and reposition physical stores as pickup/return hubs — accepting short-term revenue pain to close the strategic gap before the shift to streaming makes the physical network irrelevant.
