# MODULE 6:ADVANCED NEGOTIATION TACTICS
## Inspired by Harvard Negotiation Project

### Executive Briefing (SCQA)

**Situation:** Every significant business outcome is determined by negotiation. Acquisitions, partnerships, vendor contracts, employment offers, board decisions, capital raises — all are negotiations. Even internal resource allocation involves negotiation between competing priorities and stakeholders.

**Complication:** Most people negotiate badly. They enter negotiations with fixed positions rather than a clear understanding of their interests. They leave value on the table by treating every negotiation as zero-sum. They anchor poorly, make unnecessary concessions, and fail to create the conditions for deals that expand the total value available.

**Question:** How do executives develop the analytical discipline and interpersonal capability to negotiate effectively — whether in a high-stakes M&A transaction, a major vendor contract, or a sensitive talent conversation?

**Answer:** By mastering the Harvard Negotiation Project's principled negotiation approach: separating interests from positions, developing a strong BATNA, identifying the ZOPA, using value-creating trades, and deploying an anchoring and concession strategy that extracts maximum value while preserving the relationship.

### Core Framework 1: Interests vs. Positions

Roger Fisher and William Ury's foundational insight in *Getting to Yes* (1981): negotiators argue over positions when they should be exploring interests.

**Position:** What you say you want. The stated demand. "I want $2 million for this company."

**Interest:** Why you want it. The underlying need or motivation. "I need enough to pay off my debts, fund my next venture, and feel that my years of work were valued."

Positions are often incompatible. Interests are often compatible — or at least reconcilable.

**The orange example (Fisher and Ury's classic):**
Two children want the same orange. Their positions are incompatible — each wants the whole orange. A naive negotiator splits the orange in half. But if you ask why each child wants the orange, you discover: one wants the juice, the other wants the rind to make a cake. Their interests are perfectly compatible. Both can have exactly what they want — but only if you ask the right question.

**In practice:**
Before entering any significant negotiation, conduct an interest analysis for both sides:
- What does the other party say they want? (Their position)
- Why do they want it? (Their interests)
- What constraints are they operating under that may not be visible?
- What would a deal that fully satisfied their interests look like?

### Core Framework 2: BATNA — Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement

Your BATNA is what you will do if the current negotiation fails. It is your outside option. It is the single most important factor in determining your negotiating power.

**The BATNA principle:** Never agree to anything worse than your BATNA. Your BATNA is your reservation point — the minimum acceptable outcome from the negotiation.

**Why BATNA matters:**
- A weak BATNA (no good alternative) puts you in a weak negotiating position. You need this deal more than the other party does, which they will sense and exploit.
- A strong BATNA (multiple good alternatives) gives you negotiating power. You can walk away credibly, which changes the entire dynamic.

**BATNA development is a strategic activity:**
Before entering a major negotiation, invest time in developing your BATNA. If you're selling a company, run a competitive auction process — the other bidders improve your BATNA. If you're negotiating a job offer, have other offers in hand. If you're negotiating a supplier contract, develop alternative suppliers before the negotiation begins.

**Estimating the other party's BATNA:**
Equally important — and often neglected. If you understand the other party's outside options, you understand their floor. A seller with no other buyers is in a very different position from a seller with three competing bids. Understanding the other party's BATNA tells you how hard you can push.

### Core Framework 3: ZOPA — Zone of Possible Agreement

The ZOPA is the range of outcomes that both parties would prefer to their BATNAs. A deal is possible only if a ZOPA exists.

**Mapping the ZOPA:**
- Your reservation point: the worst outcome you would accept (determined by your BATNA)
- Their reservation point: the worst outcome they would accept (determined by their BATNA)
- If your reservation point and their reservation point overlap, a ZOPA exists

**Example — Company acquisition:**
- Seller's reservation point: $80M (their BATNA — best offer from other acquirer, or continue operating independently)
- Buyer's reservation point: $120M (their BATNA — build the capability internally, or acquire a different company)
- ZOPA: $80M — $120M (any deal in this range makes both parties better off than their BATNA)

**The negotiation within the ZOPA:**
Once you've established that a ZOPA exists, the negotiation is about where within the ZOPA the deal lands. This is where anchoring, concession strategy, and relationship dynamics determine the outcome.

### Core Framework 4: Anchoring

The first number offered in a negotiation has a disproportionate influence on the final outcome. This is anchoring — one of the most robust findings in behavioral economics.

**Why anchoring works:**
The human brain uses the first piece of information it receives as a reference point for subsequent judgments. Even when people consciously try to adjust from an anchor, they typically adjust insufficiently.

**Anchoring principles:**

1. **Anchor first when you have information advantage.** If you know the other party's reservation point is lower than your ask, anchor first at an ambitious level.

2. **Anchor aggressively but credibly.** An anchor outside the range of credibility (too high or too low) is rejected, which can damage the negotiation. The anchor must be aggressive but defensible.

3. **When the other party anchors first, counter-anchor.** Do not simply counter their anchor with a small adjustment — this accepts their frame. Counter with your own anchor and your own justification.

4. **Use objective criteria to justify your anchor.** "I am asking $X because comparable transactions in this market have closed at $Y times EBITDA, and our EBITDA is $Z" is more powerful than "I am asking $X because that's what I want."

### Core Framework 5: Creating Value Through Trades

The most sophisticated negotiators don't just fight over position — they find ways to expand the total value available. This is possible when parties value different things differently.

**The value creation principle:** If you can identify issues where you value one outcome and they value a different outcome, you can trade on those issues, making both parties better off.

**Example — M&A transaction:**
A startup founder wants a high acquisition price. The acquiring company wants to minimize cash outlay. At first glance this is zero-sum. But:
- The acquirer also wants the founder to stay and lead the acquired unit for three years (retention)
- The founder also wants autonomy, a meaningful title, and upside if the acquired unit performs well

Trade structure: Accept a lower upfront cash price in exchange for an earnout tied to performance, continued leadership of the unit, and meaningful equity in the parent company. Both parties get more of what they value most.

**Identifying trade opportunities:**
Ask the other party to rank the issues in the negotiation by importance to them. Where your rankings differ, trades are possible. The issues they value most but you value least are the ones to give on. The issues you value most but they value least are the ones to push on.

### Case Study: Disney's Acquisition of 21st Century Fox (2017-2019)

#### The Setup

In December 2017, Disney announced it would acquire 21st Century Fox's entertainment assets for $52.4 billion in stock. The assets included: 20th Century Fox film and TV studios, FX Networks, National Geographic, Fox's 30% stake in Hulu, and significant international assets including Star India.

This was not a simple negotiation. Multiple parties had interests at stake. Comcast was a competing bidder. The Fox family (the Murdochs) had complex succession and legacy considerations. Regulatory approval in multiple jurisdictions was required. And the Hulu stake required negotiating with existing Hulu partners (including Comcast, itself a competing bidder for Fox).

#### The Interest Analysis

**Disney's interests (underlying the position):**
- Content library to support Disney+ launch (the core strategic rationale)
- Hulu stake to address streaming for adult audiences (Disney brand won't carry R-rated content)
- International distribution capabilities (particularly Star India's 700M+ addressable audience)
- Eliminate a major content competitor in the studio system

**Fox/Murdoch family interests (underlying their position):**
- Maximize value for shareholders
- Exit the entertainment business and focus on news assets (Fox News, Fox Sports) — Rupert Murdoch's strategic conviction that entertainment was a scale business they couldn't win
- Protect the Murdoch family's legacy and control of remaining Fox assets
- Find a buyer who would be a good steward of the Fox creative legacy

**Where interests aligned:** Disney could pay a premium because the assets were uniquely valuable to them (streaming strategy), and Fox's family wanted a credible buyer who would preserve the assets — Disney's brand promise matched that interest.

**Where interests diverged:** Price (Disney wanted to pay as little as possible; Fox wanted maximum value). Speed (regulatory approval was uncertain; Fox wanted certainty of close).

#### The Comcast Dynamic

Comcast made a competing bid of $65 billion in cash in June 2018 — a significant premium over Disney's stock offer. This dramatically improved Fox's BATNA and, by extension, Fox's negotiating power.

Disney responded by raising its offer to $71.3 billion — a 38% increase from the original offer. The Comcast bidding war cost Disney approximately $20 billion in additional consideration.

**The negotiation lesson:** Fox's BATNA was transformed by Comcast's entry into the auction. Fox negotiated from a position of strength precisely because they had a credible alternative. Disney had to pay for that BATNA.

#### Key Terms and Value Trades

The final deal included several value-creating structures beyond the headline price:
- Disney assumed $13.7 billion in Fox debt (made the equity price appear lower while fully compensating Fox)
- Spinoff of "new Fox" (news and sports assets) before close — preserving Murdoch family control of the assets they wanted to keep
- Regulatory divestitures (Fox's regional sports networks sold separately) — shared cost of regulatory risk
- International regulatory approvals navigated jointly
