# MODULE 10:ENTREPRENEURIAL FINANCE
## Inspired by Columbia Business School

### Executive Briefing (SCQA)

**Situation:** Capital is the oxygen of a startup. Without it, even the most brilliant business model cannot survive long enough to prove itself. The mechanics of how startups raise capital — from the first friends-and-family check to a Series C venture round — determine not just whether a company survives but who owns it, who controls it, and who captures the value it creates.

**Complication:** Most founders are brilliant at building products but underprepared for the financial and legal mechanics of fundraising. They give away too much equity too early, accept terms that cede control, structure cap tables that create problems in future rounds, and make valuation decisions without understanding how those decisions compound over time.

**Question:** How do founders and early-stage investors evaluate funding opportunities, structure capital raises efficiently, maintain governance control while bringing in outside capital, and think about valuation in the absence of the financial metrics that traditional DCF analysis requires?

**Answer:** By mastering the full arc of startup finance — from bootstrapping through venture rounds — including the mechanics of term sheets, the mathematics of dilution, the dynamics of VC fund economics, and the valuation methodologies specific to early-stage companies.

### Core Framework 1: The Startup Funding Lifecycle

**Stage 0: Bootstrapping**
The company is funded entirely by the founder's personal capital, early revenue, or both. No outside investors. Full ownership and control.

*When appropriate:* The business model generates early revenue, capital requirements are low, or the founder has sufficient personal resources. Many SaaS businesses, consulting firms, and consumer internet businesses have been bootstrapped to significant scale.

*Strategic advantage of bootstrapping:* Full ownership, no investor pressure, and the discipline that comes from capital constraint. Constraints force creativity — companies that can't spend their way out of problems often find better solutions.

*When bootstrapping fails:* When the business requires significant capital before revenue (biotech, hardware, marketplace businesses requiring simultaneous supply and demand acquisition), bootstrapping is not viable.

**Stage 1: Friends, Family, and Angels ($25K–$2M)**
Early-stage capital from the founder's network and professional angel investors. Typically converts to equity through a SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) or convertible note.

*SAFE mechanics:* Y Combinator introduced the SAFE in 2013. A SAFE is not a loan — it doesn't bear interest and doesn't have a maturity date. It is an agreement to convert to equity at a future priced round, typically with a discount (20%) or valuation cap that protects early investors.

*Convertible note mechanics:* A loan that converts to equity at the next priced round. Bears interest (typically 5-8% per year). Has a maturity date. Includes a discount rate and/or valuation cap similar to a SAFE.

**Stage 2: Seed Round ($500K–$5M)**
Institutional seed capital from dedicated seed funds (First Round, Founder Collective, Precursor), accelerators (Y Combinator, Techstars), or larger VCs leading small seed rounds. Typically a priced round with preferred equity, or SAFEs at higher amounts.

*What seed investors want to see:* A compelling founding team, evidence of a large market, early product-market fit signals, a differentiated insight about the market.

**Stage 3: Series A ($5M–$25M)**
The first major institutional round. Led by a Tier 1 or Tier 2 VC fund. The company now has a priced preferred equity structure with detailed term sheet provisions.

*What Series A investors want to see:* Product-market fit evidence (retention, NPS, cohort analysis), initial revenue traction, a clear growth model, and a credible path to the metrics required for Series B.

**Stage 4: Series B and Beyond ($20M–$100M+)**
Growth capital to scale a proven model. Higher bar — investors want to see the unit economics work, the growth is predictable, and the market opportunity is large enough to justify the valuation.

**Stage 5: Late Stage / Pre-IPO ($100M–$500M+)**
Large rounds from growth equity funds (Tiger Global historically, Coatue, General Atlantic) or strategic investors. Company is typically several years from an IPO or considering strategic M&A.

**Stage 6: Liquidity Events — IPO or M&A**
The founder and early investors realize value. IPO creates a public market for the company's shares. M&A provides immediate liquidity at a negotiated price.

### Core Framework 2: VC Fund Economics

Understanding how VC funds make money is essential for founders — it explains why VCs behave the way they do.

**The fund structure:**
A VC fund raises capital from limited partners (LPs) — institutional investors (pension funds, endowments, family offices) and high-net-worth individuals. The general partners (GPs) manage the fund, make investment decisions, and receive two forms of compensation:

**Management fee:** Typically 2% of committed capital per year. Pays the firm's operating expenses (salaries, office, travel). Does not depend on investment performance.

**Carried interest ("carry"):** Typically 20% of profits above a hurdle rate. This is how GPs get wealthy — if the fund returns 3x on a $500M fund, the GPs receive 20% of the profits (20% × $1B profits = $200M in carry).

**The power law of VC returns:**
VC portfolio returns follow a power law distribution — a small number of investments generate the majority of returns, while most investments fail or return less than invested. The data consistently shows that:
- Approximately 50% of VC investments return less than the invested capital
- Approximately 25% return 1-3x
- Approximately 20% return 3-10x
- Approximately 5% return 10x+

The extraordinary outcome — the 10x+ investment — is required to make the overall fund economics work. A $500M fund needs at least one company that returns $250M+ just to pay back the fund. The unicorn (or decacorn) is not a nice-to-have; it is a mathematical necessity.

**The implication for founders:** VCs are rationally uninterested in modest outcomes. A company that sells for $50M is a marginal outcome for a VC fund — it might return 1-2x on a small investment but it doesn't move the needle for the fund. VCs want the bet on the very large outcome, even at higher risk. This creates a structural tension with founders who might rationally prefer a lower-risk, smaller exit.

### Core Framework 3: Valuation Methodologies for Early-Stage Companies

Traditional DCF is not useful for early-stage companies — you can't discount cash flows that don't exist and won't exist for years. Alternative methodologies:

**The Venture Capital Method (Bill Sahlman, HBS):**

Step 1: Estimate the company's value at exit (typically 5-7 years)
Using comparable public companies or comparable M&A transactions, estimate what the company will be worth if it hits its targets.

Step 2: Apply the required return
VCs target a multiple of invested capital (MOIC) — often 10x for Seed/Series A given the high failure rate. If the exit value is $500M and the VC wants 10x, the post-money valuation today is $500M ÷ 10 = $50M.

Step 3: Determine the pre-money valuation
If the VC is investing $5M and the post-money valuation is $50M, the pre-money valuation is $45M, and the VC owns $5M/$50M = 10% of the company post-investment.

**Comparable transactions:**
Value the company relative to similar companies that have been funded or acquired recently. If comparable SaaS companies at Series A are being valued at 10x ARR, and your ARR is $1M, your Series A valuation might be approximately $10M.

**The Scorecard Method (Bill Payne):**
Used for pre-revenue companies at the angel/seed stage. Start with an average valuation for comparable companies in the same stage and region, then adjust based on factors like team quality, market size, product stage, competitive environment, and additional risk factors.

**Revenue multiples:**
For companies with meaningful revenue, valuation is often expressed as a multiple of ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) or revenue. The multiple depends on growth rate, retention, market size, and competitive position. High-growth SaaS companies have historically traded at 10-30x ARR; slower-growth companies at 3-8x.

### Core Framework 4: Cap Table Management and Dilution

The capitalization table (cap table) records who owns what percentage of the company. Managing the cap table is one of the most important responsibilities of a founder.

**The dilution mechanics:**

At founding, the founders own 100%. Each funding round creates new shares, diluting all existing shareholders proportionally.

**Example cap table evolution:**

*At founding:*
- Founder A: 60%
- Founder B: 40%

*After option pool creation (15%) and Seed ($500K at $4M pre-money):*
- New total shares increase; existing shareholders are diluted
- Investor owns $500K / $4.5M (post-money) = 11.1%
- Option pool: 15%
- Founders together own: 73.9%

*After Series A ($5M at $20M pre-money):*
- Series A investor owns $5M / $25M (post-money) = 20%
- All existing shareholders diluted by 20%
- Founders' combined ownership: approximately 59%

*After Series B ($20M at $80M pre-money):*
- Series B investor owns $20M / $100M (post-money) = 20%
- All existing shareholders diluted by 20%
- Founders' combined ownership: approximately 47%

**The lesson:** Dilution is inevitable and not inherently bad. Owning 20% of a $500M company is far better than owning 100% of a $5M company. The question is not "how do I minimize dilution?" but "is the capital being raised worth the dilution?"

**Key term sheet provisions that affect economics:**

**Liquidation preference:** VCs receive their investment back before common shareholders (founders, employees) receive anything in a liquidation event. A 1x non-participating preference means investors get 1x their investment back before proceeds flow to common shareholders. A participating preference means investors get their investment back AND participate in remaining proceeds — a double dip that significantly reduces founder economics.

**Anti-dilution protection:** Protects investors against down rounds (fundraising at a lower valuation than previous rounds). Full ratchet anti-dilution (rare) gives investors the right to receive additional shares to maintain their percentage ownership — can be extremely founder-unfavorable. Broad-based weighted average anti-dilution (standard) provides a more moderate adjustment.

**Pro-rata rights:** The right to participate in future rounds to maintain ownership percentage. Protects investors from dilution in future rounds.

### Case Study: Airbnb's Funding Journey — From Cereal to $100B

#### The Setup

In 2008, Brian Chesky, Joe Gebbia, and Nathan Blecharczyk founded Airbnb in San Francisco. The idea: let people rent air mattresses in their apartments to conference attendees when hotels were full. The name: AirBed & Breakfast.

The founding story is one of the most celebrated in Silicon Valley. In the early days, the company was so short of money that Chesky and Gebbia created limited-edition cereal boxes — "Obama O's" and "Cap'n McCains" — to coincide with the 2008 presidential election. They sold $30,000 worth of cereal and used the cash to keep the company alive.

Y Combinator's Paul Graham initially dismissed the idea: "People are doing this? Why?" But after meeting the founders, he was convinced by their resourcefulness and execution capability. YC accepted Airbnb into the Winter 2009 batch.

#### The Funding Timeline

**Y Combinator (Winter 2009):** $20,000 for approximately 6% equity (standard YC terms at the time). At this valuation (~$333K pre-money), the entire company was worth less than a San Francisco studio apartment.

**Seed round (April 2009):** $600,000 at a $2.4M pre-money valuation ($3M post-money). Led by Sequoia Capital and Y Ventures. The company now had enough runway to test its model seriously.

**Series A (November 2010):** $7.2M at approximately $65M pre-money valuation. Led by Greylock Partners and Sequoia. By this point, the company had demonstrated meaningful traction — hosts and guests were accumulating and the business was scaling.

**Series B (July 2011):** $112M at a $1B post-money valuation. Led by Andreessen Horowitz. Airbnb became a unicorn four years after founding. The Series B funded aggressive international expansion.

**Pre-IPO rounds (2014-2020):** Multiple rounds raising hundreds of millions at escalating valuations, peaking at a $31B valuation in a 2017 private round.

**IPO (December 2020):** Airbnb went public at $68 per share, closing its first day of trading at $144.71 — a market cap of approximately $86 billion. At the time, the founders still held significant ownership stakes.

#### The Valuation Journey

The progression from $333K pre-money at YC acceptance to $86B at IPO represents a 250,000x increase in 11 years. Each funding round was predicated on evidence that reduced uncertainty:

- Seed: The idea is validated — people will rent their homes and people will stay in strangers' homes
- Series A: The model is working — unit economics are positive, retention is strong
- Series B: The model is scalable — growth in new markets follows the same pattern
- Late stages: The model is a winner — Airbnb is the dominant player in a massive market

#### The Entrepreneurial Finance Lessons

**Lesson 1: Resourcefulness as a signal**
The cereal story is not about cereal. It is about the founders' willingness to do non-scalable, creative things to survive long enough to prove their model. Paul Graham's maxim: "Do things that don't scale." The investors who backed Airbnb early were betting on the founders as much as the business.

**Lesson 2: Valuation is a negotiation, not a calculation**
Each of Airbnb's early rounds was priced by negotiation between founders and investors with asymmetric information. The "right" valuation didn't exist — what existed was a zone of possible agreement between what the founders were willing to accept and what investors were willing to pay.

**Lesson 3: Dilution math compounds**
The founders who owned 100% at founding owned significantly less at IPO — after the option pool, multiple funding rounds, and secondary sales. But the value per percentage point had increased by orders of magnitude. The decision to raise each round and accept each dilution was rational because the capital fueled growth that increased the value of each remaining share.
