## 1. The Economics of the Fund

Before understanding how Private Equity (PE) or Venture Capital (VC) firms invest in companies, you must understand how they make money. They raise capital from Limited Partners (LPs), such as university endowments and pension funds, and manage it as General Partners (GPs).

### The "2 and 20" Model
Historically, alternatives funds operate on a fee structure of:
- **2% Management Fee:** Calculated on assets under management (AUM). This pays for the salaries, offices, and operations of the firm regardless of performance.
- **20% Carried Interest ("Carry"):** A cut of the profits generated above a certain hurdle rate. This is the financial windfall for successful investments.

## 2. Venture Capital Mechanics

VC is the business of outliers. Most early-stage companies fail. A VC fund’s returns are typically driven by the "Power Law", where 1 or 2 investments out of an entire portfolio generate 100% of the fund’s profits.

### Term Sheets & Capital Structure
When VCs invest, they do not just buy common stock; they buy **Preferred Stock**, which comes with crucial economic and control rights:
- **Liquidation Preference:** In a sub-optimal exit (e.g., selling the company for less than its valuation), preferred shareholders get their money back *first*, before founders or employees see a dime.
- **Anti-Dilution Provisions:** Protects investors if the company issues shares at a lower price in the future (a "Down Round").
- **Board Seats & Protective Provisions:** Grants minority VC investors veto power over major corporate actions (debt issuance, M&A, executive hiring).

## 3. Private Equity & Leveraged Buyouts (LBOs)

While VC bets on hyper-growth and innovation, traditional Private Equity bets on cash flows, operational efficiency, and debt.

### The Leveraged Buyout
In an LBO, a PE firm essentially buys a mature, cash-generating company using a massive amount of borrowed money (leverage). The debt is secured against the acquired company's own assets, and the acquired company's cash flows pay off the debt over time.

**The Strategy:**
1. Buy a company for $100M using $20M of PE equity and $80M of debt.
2. Improve operations and use the company's cash flow to pay down the debt to $40M over 5 years.
3. Sell the company 5 years later for $140M.
4. After paying off the $40M remaining debt, the PE firm keeps $100M. They turned their initial $20M into $100M—a 5x return—purely through the mechanics of leverage and mild equity appreciation.

### Executive Takeaway
Private markets are defined by asymmetrical information and aggressive structuring. Founders must realize that taking venture capital is not just accepting cash; it is strapping a rocket engine to your company with a strictly defined timeline for an exit. PE executives must master the precise balance of maximum debt utility against operational implosion.
