# MODULE 14: VENTURE CAPITAL & PRIVATE EQUITY
## Inspired by The Wharton School

### Executive Briefing (SCQA)

**Situation:** Private capital markets — venture capital, private equity, growth equity, and leveraged buyouts — allocate trillions of dollars of capital annually. They fund the formation of new companies, the transformation of existing ones, and the transfer of ownership across the economy. Understanding how these markets work is essential for any executive, whether they are seeking capital for a growing business, evaluating a potential acquisition, or managing an organization that is itself the target of private capital.

**Complication:** Private markets are opaque and counter-intuitive. VC fund economics create systematic preference for extreme outcomes rather than good ones. Private equity creates powerful incentives toward short-term value extraction that can be misaligned with long-term organizational health. And the mathematics of dilution and liquidation preferences create complex, multi-dimensional agreements that distribute eventual proceeds in ways that are rarely intuitive.

**Question:** How do executives understand the mechanics, incentives, and economics of private capital — well enough to engage intelligently with investors, negotiate terms that preserve their interests, and make capital allocation decisions that create rather than destroy long-term value?

**Answer:** By mastering the structural mechanics of VC and PE funds, the mathematics of common deal terms (dilution, liquidation preferences, anti-dilution protections), the valuation methodologies specific to private companies, and the strategic implications of different capital structures.

### Core Framework 1: The Venture Capital Model

#### Fund Structure and LP/GP Economics

A venture capital fund is a pooled investment vehicle with a well-defined structure:

**Limited Partners (LPs):** The capital providers — institutional investors (university endowments, pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, corporate pension plans) and wealthy individuals who commit capital to the fund for investment.

**General Partners (GPs):** The investment managers who run the fund — the name-brand VC partners who source deals, make investment decisions, and manage portfolio companies. GPs commit a small percentage of fund capital themselves (typically 1-2%) as "skin in the game."

**Fund Economics:**

**Management fee:** The GP charges LPs a management fee — typically 2% of committed capital per year during the investment period (years 1-5), reducing to 2% of invested capital during the harvest period (years 6-10). On a $500 million fund, this generates $10 million per year in management fee revenue, covering the firm's operating expenses regardless of investment performance.

**Carried interest ("carry"):** The GP's share of investment profits — typically 20% of profits above the fund's "preferred return" or "hurdle rate" (typically 8% per year). If a $500 million fund returns $1.5 billion (a 3x return), the total profit is $1 billion. After returning the preferred return (8% annually) to LPs, the GP receives 20% of the remaining profit. On a 3x fund, this carry can easily exceed $150-200 million.

**The incentive implication:** GPs make money from management fees regardless of performance; they get wealthy only through carry. This creates rational pressure toward fund sizes (larger management fees) and return profiles (very large exits, not moderate-performing companies) that may not always align with individual founder or company outcomes.

#### The Power Law of VC Returns

Venture capital portfolio returns follow a highly skewed distribution. Large-scale studies of VC fund returns consistently show:

- Approximately 50% of investments return less than 1x invested capital (failures or disappointing exits)
- Approximately 25% return 1-3x (adequate but not fund-making)
- Approximately 20% return 3-10x (good outcomes)
- Approximately 5% return 10x+ (exceptional outcomes)

This 5% of investments typically generates 50-70% of the total fund's returns.

**The mathematical implication:** A $500M VC fund must target at least $1.5B+ in total returns to deliver acceptable returns to LPs after fees and carried interest. If the fund makes 50 investments averaging $10M each, it needs at least 2-3 companies to return $250M+ (a 25x+ return on a $10M investment) just to cover the fund.

**What this means for founders:** VCs cannot, by their fund economics, be interested in companies that will sell for $30-50 million. That outcome might be life-changing for a founder but is irrelevant to a VC fund. VCs must fund companies that have at least a plausible pathway to a $500M+ valuation — and invest in them at valuations that make the return math work.

#### The Venture Investment Decision Process

**Sourcing:** VCs source deals through warm referrals (introductions from other founders, co-investors, advisors), proactive outreach to interesting companies, and inbound applications. Top-tier VCs receive thousands of pitches per year and fund fewer than 1%.

**Initial screening:** Most pitches are rejected within days based on a quick assessment: Is this market large enough? Does this team have the right background? Is the product differentiated? Is the business model sensible at scale?

**Due diligence:** For companies that pass initial screening, VCs conduct 4-8 weeks of detailed investigation:
- Market diligence: Independent analysis of market size, competitive dynamics, regulatory environment
- Technical diligence: Assessment of the product, technology stack, engineering team quality
- Reference checks: Conversations with customers, former employees, co-founders
- Financial model review: Assessment of unit economics, growth assumptions, capital requirements
- Legal diligence: IP ownership, employment contracts, existing cap table, regulatory compliance

**Investment committee:** The final investment decision is made by a committee of senior partners. The investing partner presents the opportunity; the committee votes.

### Core Framework 2: Venture Capital Term Sheet Mechanics

The term sheet is the document that governs the economics and governance of a VC investment. Understanding its key provisions is essential for any founder.

#### Valuation and Ownership

**Pre-money valuation:** The value of the company before the investment is made.

**Post-money valuation:** Pre-money valuation + investment amount.

**Example:**
- Pre-money valuation: $18 million
- Investment: $2 million
- Post-money valuation: $20 million
- Investor ownership: $2M / $20M = 10%

**Option pool:** Most term sheets require the creation or expansion of an employee stock option pool — unissued shares reserved for future employee grants. The critical detail: VCs typically require the option pool to be created on a pre-money basis, meaning the dilution from creating the option pool is absorbed by founders and existing shareholders, not shared with the new investor.

**Option pool dilution example:**
- Founders' pre-investment ownership: 100%
- Term sheet: $2M on $18M pre-money, requires 15% option pool on a pre-money basis
- After option pool creation (before investment): Founders own 85%, option pool is 15%
- After $2M investment at $18M pre-money (now $20M post-money): Founders own 72%, option pool 12.75%, investor 10%
- Founders' effective dilution from this single round: 28%

#### Liquidation Preferences

One of the most economically significant provisions for founders. Liquidation preference determines who gets paid first if the company is sold, and how much.

**1x non-participating preference:** Standard market terms. The investor receives the greater of (a) 1x their invested capital OR (b) their pro-rata share of proceeds if converted to common stock. This protects investors against downside scenarios without allowing them to "double dip."

**Example:** $5M invested at 10% ownership. Company sells for $30M.
- If investor takes preference: receives $5M (liquidation preference)
- If investor converts: receives 10% × $30M = $3M
- Investor will take preference ($5M > $3M)
- Founders/employees receive $25M

**Example:** Company sells for $80M.
- If investor takes preference: receives $5M (liquidation preference)
- If investor converts: receives 10% × $80M = $8M
- Investor converts ($8M > $5M)
- Founders/employees receive $72M

**1x participating preference ("double dip"):** Investor receives 1x invested capital FIRST, then participates pro-rata in the remaining proceeds. This is highly investor-favorable and should be negotiated against when possible.

**Example:** $5M invested at 10% ownership. Company sells for $30M.
- Investor receives $5M preference PLUS 10% of remaining $25M = $5M + $2.5M = **$7.5M total**
- Founders/employees receive $22.5M (vs. $25M under non-participating)

**Participating preference cap:** A negotiated limit on the participation. "1x participating, capped at 3x" means the investor double-dips but stops when they've received 3x their investment. Founder-favorable compared to uncapped participation.

#### Anti-Dilution Protection

Anti-dilution protections defend investors against "down rounds" — future fundraising at a lower valuation than the current round.

**Broad-based weighted average anti-dilution:** The most common and most founder-friendly form. The conversion price of preferred shares is adjusted downward (meaning investors receive more shares upon conversion) based on a formula that considers both the new share price and the number of shares being issued.

**Full ratchet anti-dilution:** The most aggressive form. The conversion price is adjusted to match the price of any new shares issued, regardless of how many. If a Series A occurred at $10/share and a Series B occurs at $5/share, full ratchet gives Series A investors the right to receive twice as many shares — as if they had invested at $5/share. This can be severely dilutive to founders in down round scenarios and should be strongly negotiated against.

### Core Framework 3: Private Equity and the Leveraged Buyout

Private equity (PE) is fundamentally different from venture capital in its target companies, investment thesis, and value creation mechanisms.

**VC targets:** Early-stage companies with unproven business models, high growth potential, high failure rates.

**PE targets:** Mature companies with stable, predictable cash flows, often underperforming relative to their potential due to management quality, operational inefficiency, or capital allocation issues.

#### The LBO Model — How Private Equity Math Works

A Leveraged Buyout is an acquisition financed primarily with debt secured against the acquired company's assets and cash flows. The PE firm contributes a relatively small equity investment and finances the majority of the purchase price with borrowed money.

**The fundamental LBO insight:** Debt is cheap relative to equity returns. If you can finance an acquisition with 60-70% debt at 7-8% interest and the business generates 12-15% returns on its assets, the equity investors earn dramatically higher returns than they would if they finance entirely with equity.

**Step-by-step LBO:**

**Step 1: Identify the target**
PE firms target companies with:
- Stable, predictable free cash flows (to service the debt)
- A dominant or defensible market position (to protect future cash flows)
- Operational improvement opportunities (to increase cash generation)
- Potential exit options (strategic buyers, IPO alternatives)

**Step 2: Structure the financing**

*Example acquisition:*
- Purchase price: $500 million (at 10x EBITDA, with the company generating $50M EBITDA)
- Debt financing: $350 million (senior secured at 7%, high-yield at 10%)
- PE equity: $150 million

**Step 3: Create value during the hold period (typically 3-7 years)**

PE firms create value through multiple levers:
- **Operational improvement:** Cost reduction, revenue enhancement, management strengthening
- **Organic growth:** Expansion into adjacent markets, geographic expansion
- **Add-on acquisitions:** Buying smaller companies to add scale or capabilities
- **Financial engineering:** Optimizing the capital structure, reducing interest costs as the debt is paid down

**Step 4: Exit — Realize the investment**

*Example exit (5 years later):*
- EBITDA has grown from $50M to $80M through operational improvements
- Debt has been paid down from $350M to $200M through cash generation
- Exit multiple: 11x EBITDA (modest multiple expansion)
- Enterprise value at exit: $880M
- Total proceeds: $880M - $200M debt = $680M to equity holders
- PE firm's return: $680M / $150M equity invested = **4.5x return (34% IRR)**

**The leverage effect:** Without leverage, the same operational improvement (EBITDA from $50M to $80M, same multiple expansion) on a fully equity-financed deal would have generated approximately $250M in value on a $500M investment — a 1.5x return. Leverage amplified the equity return from 1.5x to 4.5x.

**The risk of leverage:** The same leverage that amplifies gains also amplifies losses. If EBITDA declines from $50M to $30M due to a recession, the debt becomes unsustainable. With $350M of debt and a company only generating $30M in EBITDA, debt service consumes all operating cash flow and the equity is worthless. Several PE deals have ended in bankruptcy — not because the underlying business was terminally broken, but because the leverage structure could not survive a deteriorating operating environment.

#### Value Creation in Private Equity

Beyond the financial engineering, sophisticated PE firms generate returns through genuine operational improvement. The three primary value creation levers:

**Operational improvement:**
- Professionalizing management (replacing inadequate executives with experienced operators)
- Implementing rigorous performance management systems
- Rationalizing the cost structure (consolidating facilities, eliminating redundant functions)
- Improving procurement practices
- Implementing best practices across the portfolio

**Revenue enhancement:**
- Pricing optimization (many PE-acquired companies are underpriced relative to their value)
- Sales force effectiveness improvement
- Geographic or product expansion
- Digital transformation to improve customer acquisition and retention

**Strategic repositioning:**
- Divesting non-core business units to focus on the most attractive segments
- Completing add-on acquisitions to build scale or expand capabilities
- Repositioning the brand or product line to command premium pricing

### Core Framework 4: PE/VC Deal Analytics

#### Due Diligence Framework

Before investing in any private company, a rigorous due diligence process investigates:

**Market due diligence:**
- Market size and growth rate (independent verification, not just management's claims)
- Competitive dynamics (Porter's Five Forces applied to the specific market)
- Customer concentration and quality
- Regulatory risk

**Financial due diligence:**
- Quality of earnings (is reported EBITDA sustainable? What's being normalized or excluded?)
- Working capital analysis (historical patterns and future requirements)
- Capital expenditure requirements (what will the company need to spend to sustain its business?)
- Debt structure (what existing obligations must survive the transaction?)

**Operational due diligence:**
- Operational efficiency relative to industry benchmarks
- IT systems and infrastructure quality
- Supply chain risks and dependencies
- Organizational capability

**Legal due diligence:**
- Intellectual property ownership and protection
- Material contracts and change-of-control provisions
- Employment and compensation obligations
- Environmental and regulatory compliance

#### IRR and MOIC — Performance Metrics

Private equity performance is measured using two complementary metrics:

**IRR (Internal Rate of Return):** The annualized return on invested capital, accounting for the timing of cash flows. A 25% IRR target means the investment is expected to return 25% per year on the equity invested. IRR is sensitive to timing — early distributions boost IRR significantly.

**MOIC (Multiple on Invested Capital):** Simply, how many times did the investment multiply? A 3x MOIC means the fund received 3x its invested capital back. MOIC is independent of timing — it captures magnitude of return regardless of when it occurred.

**Why both metrics matter:**
- An investment that returns 3x in 2 years (incredible IRR) is better than an investment that returns 3x in 8 years (mediocre IRR)
- An investment that returns 100% IRR on a 60-day flip is impressive by IRR but irrelevant to a fund's overall performance due to small capital employed
- The combination of MOIC and IRR tells the complete story of an investment's performance

### Case Study: KKR and RJR Nabisco — The Anatomy of a Hostile Leveraged Buyout

#### The Background

In 1988, RJR Nabisco was one of the largest and most admired companies in America. The conglomerate's portfolio included Winston and Camel cigarettes (among the most profitable consumer product franchises in history) and iconic food brands including Oreo, Nabisco, Ritz, and Del Monte.

The company's operating fundamentals were extraordinary: tobacco products generated enormous, recession-resistant free cash flows. The food businesses added diversification and growth. The combined entity was generating approximately $2 billion in operating cash flow annually.

But the stock price was stagnating. Investors were skeptical of the conglomerate structure. And CEO F. Ross Johnson was known for extravagance — a fleet of corporate jets (derisively called the "RJR Air Force"), lavish executive perks, and a management culture that prioritized style over shareholder return.

#### The Management Buyout Attempt

Johnson concluded that RJR Nabisco was dramatically undervalued in the public markets. He assembled a management team and approached investment bank Shearson Lehman with a proposal: take the company private in a management buyout at $75 per share — a significant premium to the current price.

His logic was sound. At $75 per share, the company could be taken private, management could eliminate the inefficiencies of public company reporting, and the stable cash flows could pay down the acquisition debt. Executives would own equity in the private company and would capture the value they were creating.

What Johnson didn't anticipate was the reaction from KKR.

#### KKR's Entry and the Bidding War

Henry Kravis and George Roberts at KKR recognized immediately what Johnson's offer revealed: RJR Nabisco's management believed the company was worth at least $75 per share. If management was willing to pay $75, KKR concluded the company was almost certainly worth more — and that KKR, with superior operating capabilities and more aggressive financial engineering, could pay more and still earn exceptional returns.

KKR entered the auction at $90 per share, triggering a multi-week bidding war that was unprecedented in its scale and ferocity. Competing bids flew between KKR, Shearson/Johnson management group, and briefly First Boston, with each party supported by different Wall Street banks, different financing structures, and different operational plans.

**The financial engineering at the core of each bid:**

| Factor | KKR's Analysis |
|---|---|
| EBITDA (pre-acquisition) | ~$2.0 billion |
| Maximum sustainable debt | $18-20 billion (9-10x EBITDA) |
| Equity requirement | $2-3 billion |
| Maximum bid price | ~$22-25 billion total ($100-108/share) |

The winning bid: KKR ultimately prevailed at **$109 per share, or approximately $25 billion** — the largest acquisition in history at the time.

#### The Post-Acquisition Reality

KKR financed the acquisition with approximately $22 billion in debt — an extraordinary leverage level that left virtually no margin for error. The operating thesis required:

1. Aggressive cost reduction in corporate overhead (eliminating the executive jets, reducing headquarters staff dramatically)
2. Successful divestitures of non-core assets to pay down debt (Del Monte foods was sold for $1.5 billion within 18 months)
3. Continued strong performance from the tobacco business

**The operational reality was complicated:**

- The tobacco business continued generating strong cash flows, supporting debt service
- Multiple business units were sold to reduce debt: Del Monte, Nabisco's European operations, and others
- Corporate excess was eliminated: the aircraft fleet was sold, executive perks were eliminated
- But the tobacco business faced increasing litigation risk and regulatory pressure that created uncertainty about long-term cash flows

**The ultimate outcome:** KKR's own returns on RJR Nabisco were modest by PE standards — the debt load was so high that the financial engineering didn't produce the spectacular returns KKR expected. When RJR Nabisco was eventually taken public again in 1991 and the remaining stake was sold, KKR's returns were positive but below their typical targets.

The deal's legacy was not its returns — it was its cultural and regulatory impact.

#### The Enduring Lessons

**Lesson 1: Leverage amplifies both gains and losses**
RJR Nabisco showed that post-LBO outcomes often differ from underwriting models. The cash flows from tobacco supported the debt, but the complexity of managing a highly leveraged enterprise while simultaneously divesting assets and managing operational issues created a level of difficulty that affected returns.

**Lesson 2: Hostile deals are expensive**
The bidding war drove the price to the limit of financial feasibility. KKR paid virtually the maximum price the cash flows could support — leaving no margin for operational underperformance. In deals where price is determined by competitive auction, returns are often compressed.

**Lesson 3: Corporate governance vacuum creates private equity opportunity**
The deal's origin was a CEO who recognized that the public market was undervaluing his company. When public company management is not maximizing shareholder value, it creates the arbitrage opportunity that PE exploits. The best defense against a hostile PE takeover is excellent public market performance.

**Lesson 4: The cultural impact of leveraged finance**
The RJR deal, documented by Bryan Burrough and John Helyar in *Barbarians at the Gate*, crystallized a cultural moment about the role of financial capital in American business — questions that remain unresolved about the appropriate relationship between financial engineering and operational stewardship.

### Executive Takeaway

Private capital markets are not simply a financing mechanism — they are a governance mechanism. Venture capital selectively allocates risk capital to founders and business models with asymmetric return potential, funding innovation that public markets would not otherwise finance. Private equity creates a concentrated ownership structure and alignment of incentives between capital and management that public markets often cannot replicate.

Understanding the mechanics of these markets — the fund economics that shape investor behavior, the term sheet provisions that determine economic outcomes, the financial engineering that drives PE returns — is essential for any executive who will raise capital, sell a company, participate in a PE-backed portfolio, or compete against PE-owned businesses.

The most important insight: the parties on the other side of these transactions have sophisticated economic models and significant information advantages. Executives who engage without equivalent sophistication predictably achieve inferior outcomes.
