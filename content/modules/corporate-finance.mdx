# MODULE 7:CORPORATE FINANCE & VALUATION
## Inspired by University of Chicago Booth School of Business

### Executive Briefing (SCQA)

**Situation:** Every strategic decision has a financial dimension. Entering a new market, acquiring a company, launching a product, restructuring operations — all of these choices consume capital and must be evaluated against the alternatives. Finance is not accounting. Accounting records what happened. Finance helps you evaluate what should happen.

**Complication:** Most executives are comfortable with P&L management but uncomfortable with the deeper financial concepts that drive investment decisions: the time value of money, cost of capital, valuation, and capital structure. This discomfort creates blind spots in strategic decision-making — decisions that look good from a revenue perspective but destroy value from a capital allocation perspective.

**Question:** How do executives develop the financial fluency required to evaluate investment decisions rigorously, communicate with investors and boards in the language of value creation, and make capital allocation decisions that maximize long-term shareholder value?

**Answer:** By mastering five interconnected financial concepts: the time value of money (the foundation), discounted cash flow valuation (the primary tool for investment decisions), the cost of capital (the hurdle rate), capital structure (the financing decision), and the financial interpretation of M&A transactions.

### Core Framework 1: Time Value of Money

The most foundational concept in finance: a dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow.

Why? Three reasons:
1. **Opportunity cost:** A dollar today can be invested and earn a return, so it will be worth more than a dollar tomorrow
2. **Inflation:** The purchasing power of money declines over time
3. **Risk:** The future is uncertain; a promise of a dollar tomorrow carries risk that the promise won't be kept

**The mechanics:**

**Future Value:** How much will $1 invested today be worth in N years, if it earns a return of r per year?
FV = PV × (1 + r)^N

**Present Value:** How much is a payment of $1 received N years from now worth today, given a discount rate of r?
PV = FV / (1 + r)^N

**The implication for executives:** Every dollar of value created in the future is worth less than a dollar of value created today. Initiatives that defer value creation (long development cycles, slow revenue ramps) are inherently less valuable than initiatives that create value quickly — all else equal. The discount rate (r) determines exactly how much the future matters relative to the present.

### Core Framework 2: Discounted Cash Flow Valuation

DCF is the theoretically correct method for valuing any asset: a business, a project, a piece of equipment, a bond. The value of an asset is the present value of all the cash flows it will generate over its life, discounted at the appropriate rate.

**The DCF formula:**

Value = CF₁/(1+r)¹ + CF₂/(1+r)² + CF₃/(1+r)³ + ... + (CFₙ + Terminal Value)/(1+r)ⁿ

Where:
- CF = Free cash flow in each period
- r = Discount rate (WACC)
- Terminal Value = Value of cash flows beyond the explicit forecast period

**Step-by-step DCF process:**

**Step 1: Forecast free cash flow**
Free cash flow (FCF) is the cash generated by the business after funding operations and capital expenditures — the cash available to all providers of capital (debt and equity).

FCF = EBIT × (1 - tax rate) + Depreciation & Amortization - Capital Expenditures - Change in Working Capital

**Step 2: Determine the discount rate (WACC)**
(See Framework 3)

**Step 3: Calculate terminal value**
The terminal value captures the value of cash flows beyond the explicit forecast period (typically 5-10 years). Two methods:

- **Gordon Growth Model:** TV = FCF_n × (1 + g) / (WACC - g), where g is the perpetual growth rate
- **Exit multiple method:** TV = EBITDA_n × EV/EBITDA multiple (uses comparable transaction multiples)

**Step 4: Discount everything back to present**
Sum the present values of the explicit forecast period cash flows and the terminal value.

**Step 5: Subtract net debt to get equity value**
Enterprise Value - Net Debt = Equity Value
Equity Value / Shares Outstanding = Per Share Value

**The sensitivity analysis:**
DCF is highly sensitive to assumptions about the discount rate and the terminal growth rate. Responsible DCF analysis always includes a sensitivity table showing how the valuation changes across reasonable ranges of these assumptions.

### Core Framework 3: WACC — Weighted Average Cost of Capital

WACC is the blended cost of capital for the business — the return that the business must earn to justify the investment of its capital providers (both debt and equity holders).

**WACC formula:**
WACC = (E/V) × Re + (D/V) × Rd × (1 - T)

Where:
- E = Market value of equity
- D = Market value of debt
- V = E + D (total value)
- Re = Cost of equity
- Rd = Cost of debt
- T = Corporate tax rate

**Cost of Equity (Re) — CAPM:**
Re = Rf + β × (Rm - Rf)

Where:
- Rf = Risk-free rate (typically 10-year Treasury yield)
- β = Beta (how much the stock moves relative to the market)
- Rm - Rf = Equity risk premium (expected market return above the risk-free rate; historically ~5-6%)

**Cost of Debt (Rd):**
The interest rate the company pays on its debt. Debt is tax-deductible, so the after-tax cost of debt = Rd × (1 - T).

**Why WACC matters:**
WACC is the hurdle rate for investment decisions. Any investment expected to return more than WACC creates value. Any investment expected to return less than WACC destroys value. This is a simple but powerful decision rule that many organizations systematically violate.

### Core Framework 4: Capital Structure

Capital structure is the mix of debt and equity used to finance the business. The capital structure decision affects the WACC, the risk profile of the equity, and the flexibility of the business to respond to adversity.

**The Modigliani-Miller Theorem (1958):**
In a world without taxes, bankruptcy costs, or information asymmetry, capital structure is irrelevant — the value of the firm doesn't change based on how it's financed. This is the theoretical baseline.

**Why capital structure matters in the real world:**

**The tax shield:** Interest payments are tax-deductible. Debt financing creates a tax shield that reduces the effective cost of capital. The more debt, the larger the tax shield — up to a point.

**Bankruptcy costs:** Too much debt creates the risk of financial distress. Bankruptcy is expensive — legal costs, management distraction, customer and supplier concern, talent departure. As debt increases beyond an optimal level, the expected cost of financial distress begins to exceed the tax shield benefit.

**The trade-off theory:** The optimal capital structure balances the tax shield from debt against the expected cost of financial distress. In practice, the optimal capital structure varies significantly by industry:

- **Capital-intensive, stable cash flow industries (utilities, real estate):** High leverage is appropriate. Cash flows are predictable enough to service debt reliably.
- **High-growth, volatile cash flow industries (technology, biotech):** Low leverage is appropriate. Cash flow uncertainty means the risk of financial distress is high.

**The signaling theory:** Capital structure decisions send signals. A company issuing equity is often read by the market as a signal that management believes the stock is overvalued (why else issue shares?). A company doing a leveraged recapitalization signals management confidence in future cash flows.

### Core Framework 5: M&A Valuation and Merger Math

Mergers and acquisitions are the largest and most consequential capital allocation decisions most executives will make. Understanding the basic math is essential.

**The fundamental M&A question:** Does the acquisition create value?

An acquisition creates value if and only if:
Value of target to acquirer > Purchase price

The value of the target to the acquirer = standalone value of the target + synergies

**Synergy types:**
- **Revenue synergies:** Cross-selling, new markets, bundled offerings. Hard to achieve, typically over-estimated.
- **Cost synergies:** Eliminating duplicate functions, combining supply chains, consolidating facilities. More reliably achievable; typically the bulk of the value case.

**Accretion/Dilution Analysis:**

Accretion/dilution is the immediate (Year 1) impact of the acquisition on the acquirer's earnings per share (EPS).

- **Accretive acquisition:** Pro forma EPS > standalone EPS. The acquisition immediately increases earnings per share.
- **Dilutive acquisition:** Pro forma EPS < standalone EPS. The acquisition initially reduces earnings per share.

**Important caveat:** Accretion/dilution is a short-term measure. A dilutive acquisition can create enormous long-term value if the strategic rationale is strong and synergies are real. Facebook's acquisition of Instagram was dilutive at the time — the $1 billion price tag with minimal revenue was hard to justify on EPS grounds — but it was an extraordinary long-term value creation decision.

**Valuation methodologies comparison:**

| Method | What it measures | Best used for |
|---|---|---|
| DCF | Intrinsic value based on future cash flows | Capital allocation decisions |
| Comparable companies | Market value relative to peers | Sanity check, market context |
| Comparable transactions | Acquisition premiums in similar deals | M&A pricing |
| LBO analysis | Maximum price a financial buyer can pay | Benchmark for PE-backed deals |

### Case Study: Amazon's Investment Philosophy — Long-Term Value Creation

#### The Setup

Amazon has, on multiple occasions, made investment decisions that appeared to destroy near-term earnings in exchange for long-term value creation. AWS (Amazon Web Services) was built from Amazon's internal infrastructure and launched externally in 2006. Amazon Prime was launched in 2005 at a price that was demonstrably uneconomic on a per-customer basis. Amazon's fulfillment center buildout has consumed tens of billions of capital with multi-year payback periods.

By any near-term financial metric — EPS, P/E ratio, free cash flow margin — Amazon looked like a bad business for most of its first 20 years. And yet it created more value than almost any company in history.

#### The Financial Logic

Jeff Bezos articulated the financial logic clearly in his 1997 shareholder letter: "We will continue to make investment decisions in light of long-term market leadership considerations rather than short-term profitability considerations or short-term Wall Street reactions."

The financial foundation is the time value of money applied in reverse. If you believe that the future cash flows from a long-term investment will be very large, and if your cost of capital is low (Amazon's was low because investors trusted management), then you should invest aggressively even if it depresses near-term EPS.

**AWS example:**
- Investment: Hundreds of millions in infrastructure, engineering talent, and operational development over multiple years
- Near-term impact: Significant drag on operating income, negative commentary from Wall Street analysts
- Long-term outcome: AWS generates over $90 billion in annual revenue with approximately 30% operating margins, contributing more to Amazon's total operating income than its e-commerce business

The DCF math, done at the time of the investment with realistic assumptions about AWS's eventual scale, would have shown an NPV that justified the investment even at a high discount rate. But it required conviction in assumptions that were, at the time, highly uncertain.

#### The Capital Allocation Lesson

Amazon's history is a masterclass in disciplined capital allocation. Bezos consistently applied the same framework:
1. Identify a large market where Amazon can build a sustainable position
2. Invest aggressively, accepting near-term losses
3. Use the fixed-cost structure to generate operating leverage as the business scales
4. Reinvest the cash flows into the next large market

This is capital allocation as competitive strategy. The reinvestment discipline prevented the cash generation from becoming dividends or buybacks — it remained inside the flywheel, compounding.
