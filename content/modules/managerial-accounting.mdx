## 1. The Purpose of Managerial Accounting

While Financial Accounting is strictly about providing historical data to external stakeholders (via GAAP/IFRS), **Managerial Accounting** is entirely focused on providing forward-looking, actionable data to internal executives.

Financial accounting tells you *if* you made a profit. Managerial accounting tells you *where* you made it, *why*, and *how* to make more.

### 2. Activity-Based Costing (ABC)

Traditional cost accounting allocates overhead based on a simple metric (like direct labor hours). This often wildly distorts true profitability.

**Activity-Based Costing (ABC)** traces overhead costs directly to the specific activities that generate them, and then allocates those costs to products or services based on actual consumption.

- **Example:** A complex custom product and a simple mass-produced product might require the same direct labor time. Under traditional costing, they bear the same overhead. Under ABC, the custom product absorbs the high costs of specialized engineering and setup times, revealing its true (and often poor) margin.

## 3. The Balanced Scorecard

Developed by Dr. Robert Kaplan (HBS) and Dr. David Norton, the Balanced Scorecard revolutionized corporate measurement by acknowledging that strict financial metrics are lagging indicators.

The Scorecard measures a firm across four identical pillars:
1. **Financial:** "How do we look to shareholders?" (Revenue, ROI)
2. **Customer:** "How do customers see us?" (NPS, retention, satisfaction)
3. **Internal Process:** "What must we excel at?" (Cycle time, quality yields)
4. **Learning & Growth:** "How can we continue to improve and create value?" (Employee training, innovation rate)

If you only measure finance, you might maximize short-term profits by cutting R&D and customer service, silently destroying the company's long-term capability.

## 4. Incentive System Design & Control

"Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome." — Charlie Munger.

Management control systems must align the behavior of the employees with the strategic goals of the firm. Poorly designed incentives (e.g., paying sales teams entirely on revenue rather than gross margin) consistently lead to destructive optimization, where employees game the specific metric at the expense of the company.

### Executive Takeaway
You manage what you measure. Executive leadership is ultimately about designing the dashboard that your organization optimizes against, ensuring that the defined metrics are truly reflective of long-term strategic value.
