# MODULE 16: MACROECONOMICS & GLOBAL MARKETS
## Inspired by Chicago Booth School of Business

### Executive Briefing (SCQA)

**Situation:** Every business strategy is executed within a macroeconomic environment that it did not choose and cannot control. Interest rates determine the cost of capital. Inflation affects pricing power, supply costs, and consumer purchasing behavior. Currency movements determine the competitiveness of international operations. Economic cycles drive demand across virtually every industry. These forces can undermine even the most sound strategic plan.

**Complication:** Most executives think about macroeconomics only when it becomes undeniable — when central banks raise rates aggressively, when inflation spikes, when currencies move sharply, or when recession arrives. By the time macroeconomic forces are obvious, they are largely priced in to financial markets, competitive dynamics, and customer behavior. The executive who understands macroeconomic dynamics proactively has a significant advantage over one who responds reactively.

**Question:** How do executives develop sufficient macroeconomic fluency to anticipate how monetary and fiscal policy decisions affect their cost of capital and competitive environment, prepare their balance sheets for economic adversity, and make strategic decisions that create optionality in uncertain environments?

**Answer:** By mastering the core macroeconomic frameworks — the IS-LM model, the Phillips Curve, monetary and fiscal policy transmission mechanisms, and foreign exchange dynamics — and connecting them to specific business strategy and capital allocation decisions.

---

### Core Framework 1: How the Macro Environment Affects Business

Before examining specific frameworks, it is essential to understand the channels through which macroeconomic conditions affect business performance:

**Channel 1: Cost of capital**
Interest rates, set by central banks and transmitted through financial markets, determine what it costs to borrow money and (through the equity risk premium) what return equity investors require. When interest rates rise, the discount rate in a DCF increases — future cash flows are worth less in present value terms. Capital-intensive businesses, growth-stage companies with profits far in the future, and highly leveraged companies feel this most acutely.

**Channel 2: Consumer demand**
Economic growth or contraction drives the fundamental demand for most products and services. When employment is high and wages are growing, consumer spending follows. When unemployment rises and confidence falls, spending contracts. Understanding where the economy is in the business cycle enables more accurate demand forecasting.

**Channel 3: Input cost inflation**
Inflation affects not just consumer prices but all input costs: labor, raw materials, energy, logistics, professional services. Companies with significant pricing power can pass cost increases through to customers; those in commodity or competitive markets absorb the margin impact.

**Channel 4: Currency effects**
For companies with international operations, foreign exchange movements affect the translation of foreign revenue and costs into the reporting currency, the competitive position of domestically-produced goods in international markets, and the cost of imported inputs.

**Channel 5: Credit availability**
During periods of financial stress, credit markets tighten — banks reduce lending, bond markets demand higher premiums, and the availability of capital to fund operations and growth contracts. Companies with strong balance sheets can continue operating normally; overleveraged companies face existential stress.

### Core Framework 2: The IS-LM Model

The IS-LM model is the foundational framework for understanding how fiscal and monetary policy affect economic output and interest rates. It operates through two curves that simultaneously determine equilibrium GDP and interest rates.

#### The IS Curve (Investment-Saving)

The IS curve represents equilibrium in the goods market — the combinations of interest rates and income levels at which total spending (consumption + investment + government spending + net exports) equals total output.

**The key relationship:** The IS curve slopes downward. When interest rates fall:
- Businesses invest more (cheaper borrowing makes formerly marginal projects profitable)
- Consumers borrow more to buy homes and durable goods
- Government borrowing costs decline (important for fiscal policy transmission)
- Total spending increases → GDP increases

**Fiscal policy shifts the IS curve:**
- Expansionary fiscal policy (tax cuts, increased government spending) shifts the IS curve rightward — for any given interest rate, GDP is higher
- Contractionary fiscal policy (tax increases, spending cuts) shifts the IS curve leftward

#### The LM Curve (Liquidity Preference-Money Supply)

The LM curve represents equilibrium in the money market — the combinations of interest rates and income at which money demand equals money supply.

**The key relationship:** The LM curve slopes upward. When income increases:
- People and businesses demand more money for transactions
- Higher money demand (with fixed money supply) pushes interest rates up
- Higher interest rates maintain money market equilibrium

**Monetary policy shifts the LM curve:**
- Expansionary monetary policy (central bank increases money supply, cuts interest rates) shifts the LM curve rightward — for any given income level, interest rates are lower
- Contractionary monetary policy shifts the LM curve leftward

#### IS-LM Equilibrium

The economy operates where the IS and LM curves intersect — the unique combination of interest rates and GDP where both goods markets and money markets are simultaneously in equilibrium.

**Practical policy application:**

When the 2008 financial crisis caused aggregate demand to collapse (IS curve shifted sharply leftward), central banks responded by cutting interest rates to near zero (LM curve shifted rightward) and governments enacted massive fiscal stimulus programs (IS curve shifted rightward). The combined intervention partially offset the crisis-induced demand collapse.

When post-COVID inflation accelerated in 2021-2023, central banks raised interest rates aggressively (shifting LM leftward) to cool aggregate demand and reduce inflationary pressure — accepting slower growth as the cost of controlling prices.

**The executive insight:** The IS-LM framework tells you the directional effect of policy changes. When the Federal Reserve announces rate increases, the LM curve is moving leftward — growth will slow, and capital will become more expensive. Companies with high debt loads, or those planning large capital investments, need to account for this in their financial planning.

### Core Framework 3: Inflation and the Phillips Curve

#### Understanding Inflation

Inflation is the sustained increase in the general price level. It is caused by demand-side forces (too much spending chasing too few goods), supply-side forces (cost increases that producers pass through as price increases), or expectations (self-fulfilling anticipation of price increases).

**Demand-pull inflation:** Excess aggregate demand relative to the economy's productive capacity. Occurs when unemployment is low, wages are rising, consumer confidence is high, and monetary policy is loose. The 2021-2022 US inflation was partly demand-pull — pandemic fiscal stimulus increased household purchasing power dramatically.

**Cost-push inflation:** Rising input costs (energy, labor, raw materials) that producers pass through as price increases. The 1970s oil price shocks created cost-push inflation that was extremely resistant to monetary policy remedies because supply-side constraints cannot be fixed by reducing demand.

**Expectations-driven inflation:** If workers and businesses expect prices to rise, they behave in ways that cause prices to rise (workers demand higher wages, businesses raise prices preemptively). Expectations management is one of the central challenges of central banking — maintaining credibility that inflation will be controlled is essential to preventing expectations from becoming self-fulfilling.

#### The Phillips Curve

William Phillips documented an empirical relationship between unemployment and inflation in 1958: historically, low unemployment corresponded with high inflation, and high unemployment corresponded with low inflation. The implication: there is a trade-off between unemployment and inflation that policymakers must navigate.

**The traditional Phillips Curve logic:**
- Low unemployment → workers have bargaining power → wages rise → companies pass wage increases through as price increases → inflation accelerates
- High unemployment → workers have weak bargaining power → wages stagnate → companies have limited pricing power → inflation subdues

**The policymaker's dilemma:**
If the central bank wants to reduce unemployment, it can stimulate the economy (expand money supply, cut rates) — but doing so risks accelerating inflation.
If the central bank wants to reduce inflation, it can cool the economy (tighten money supply, raise rates) — but doing so risks increasing unemployment.

**The stagflation challenge:** The 1970s revealed that the simple Phillips Curve relationship can break down. Supply shocks (oil price increases) can create both high inflation and high unemployment simultaneously — "stagflation." Conventional monetary tools face an impossible dilemma: tighten to fight inflation (worsening unemployment) or loosen to fight unemployment (worsening inflation). There is no good option.

**The modern Phillips Curve debate:** Economists debate whether the Phillips Curve relationship remains stable in contemporary economies. The 2010s saw historically low unemployment alongside historically low inflation — the expected inflation acceleration didn't materialize. Structural factors (globalization, labor market shifts, technology) may have shifted the relationship. The 2021-2023 inflation surge, arriving with high employment levels, has reinvigorated the debate.

### Core Framework 4: Monetary Policy Transmission

The Federal Reserve (and equivalent central banks globally: ECB, Bank of England, Bank of Japan) controls monetary conditions primarily through the policy interest rate — the Federal Funds Rate in the US. This rate is the overnight lending rate between banks, which anchors the entire interest rate structure.

#### The Transmission Mechanism

Policy rate changes transmit through the economy through multiple channels:

**Bank lending channel:** When the Fed raises rates, banks' cost of funds increases. Banks pass this cost to borrowers through higher lending rates. Business investment and consumer borrowing decline.

**Asset price channel:** Higher interest rates reduce the present value of future cash flows for stocks, bonds, and real estate. Asset prices decline. As wealth falls, consumption contracts.

**Exchange rate channel:** Higher domestic interest rates attract foreign capital seeking higher yields. Demand for domestic currency increases, causing currency appreciation. Exports become more expensive for foreign buyers; import prices decline. Net exports contract.

**Expectations channel:** Perhaps the most important. Central bank communications signal future policy intentions. If the market believes the Fed will raise rates, interest rates across all maturities adjust immediately — even before the actual rate increases occur.

#### Unconventional Monetary Policy

When the policy rate approaches zero (or goes negative), conventional rate-cutting loses effectiveness. Central banks have deployed unconventional tools:

**Quantitative Easing (QE):** The central bank directly purchases government bonds and mortgage-backed securities, injecting reserves into the banking system and putting downward pressure on long-term interest rates. The Federal Reserve expanded its balance sheet from approximately $900 billion in 2008 to over $9 trillion by 2022 through successive rounds of QE.

**Forward guidance:** Committing to maintaining low rates for an extended period, which anchors market expectations and applies downward pressure on longer-term rates even without actual rate changes.

**Negative interest rates:** Applied by European and Japanese central banks, negative policy rates charge banks for holding excess reserves, intended to incentivize lending. The effectiveness has been debated; the impact on bank profitability has been significant.

#### Business Implications of Monetary Policy

**Zero-Interest Rate Policy (ZIRP) environment (2010-2022):**
- Cost of capital approaches zero → future cash flows discounted less heavily → growth-stage companies with profits deferred far into the future receive premium valuations
- Debt financing becomes extremely cheap → leverage increases across the corporate sector
- "Risk-free" return is near zero → investors move into risk assets seeking yield → stock prices and asset values inflate
- Zombie companies (those that can service interest but cannot repay principal) can survive and even grow

**High interest rate environment:**
- Capital becomes expensive → marginal investment projects fail required return tests
- Highly leveraged companies face acute stress as interest costs rise
- Growth company valuations decline as distant cash flows are discounted more heavily
- Short-duration assets (value stocks, dividend payers) outperform long-duration assets (growth stocks)
- Companies with strong free cash flow generation are rewarded; companies dependent on continued capital raises face existential pressure

### Core Framework 5: Foreign Exchange and International Finance

#### Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

In the long run, exchange rates should reflect the relative price levels of countries. If a dollar buys 100 units of a basket of goods in the US and the same basket costs £60 in the UK, the exchange rate should trend toward $1.67/£1 to equilibrate purchasing power.

PPP is not a reliable short-term predictor (exchange rates deviate from PPP for years) but is useful for long-term strategic analysis: companies entering markets with significantly overvalued currencies face future competitive disadvantage as currencies revert toward PPP levels.

**The Big Mac Index:** The Economist developed a simple PPP measure using McDonald's Big Mac prices as the benchmark basket — a globally standardized product with consistent ingredients across markets. While not technically rigorous, it provides an intuitive illustration of PPP deviations and has predicted long-term exchange rate movements with reasonable accuracy.

#### Interest Rate Parity

Capital flows globally toward the highest risk-adjusted return. The interest rate parity condition states that, adjusted for expected exchange rate changes, investment returns in different currencies should be equivalent in the long run.

If the US offers 5% interest rates while the Eurozone offers 2%, capital will flow toward US dollar assets. This capital flow drives dollar appreciation. The dollar will appreciate until its expected future decline (as it reverts toward fair value) offsets the interest rate differential.

**Business implication:** When the US Fed raises rates relative to the ECB, the dollar typically appreciates. US companies with significant European revenues will see those revenues translate into fewer dollars. US companies manufacturing in the US and selling in Europe will face competitiveness headwinds. US companies manufacturing in Europe and earning in Euros will see their costs (in dollar terms) decline.

#### FX Risk Management

Corporations manage foreign exchange risk through:

**Natural hedging:** Matching revenue and costs in the same currency. A company that earns euros and also employs thousands of European workers and pays European suppliers has natural hedge — exchange rate movements affect revenues and costs simultaneously.

**Forward contracts:** Agreements to buy or sell a specific amount of a foreign currency at a specified exchange rate on a future date. They lock in today's exchange rate for a future transaction, eliminating uncertainty about future cash flows in the reporting currency.

**Options:** Like forward contracts, but the company pays a premium for the right (not obligation) to transact at the specified rate. Options provide downside protection while preserving upside if exchange rates move favorably.

**Currency swaps:** Exchange of payment streams in different currencies, typically used for long-term financing in foreign currencies.

**Strategic hedging policy:** The chief treasury officer's challenge is not maximizing FX gains (that's speculation, not hedging) but minimizing the impact of exchange rate volatility on operating results and capital planning.

### Core Framework 6: Economic Cycle Analysis

Business cycle analysis — understanding where the economy is in the expansion/contraction cycle — is one of the most practically valuable macroeconomic skills for business leaders.

#### The Business Cycle Phases

**Expansion:** GDP growing, unemployment falling, consumer confidence improving, credit availability increasing. Business conditions favor growth investment. Companies that invested in capacity during contraction are positioned to gain share.

**Peak:** GDP at its cyclical high, unemployment at cyclical low, inflation accelerating, credit markets frothy. Risk of over-investment and acquisition activity at peak valuations. Prudent executives protect balance sheet strength in anticipation of the turn.

**Contraction (Recession):** GDP falling, unemployment rising, credit tightening, consumer and business confidence deteriorating. Companies with strong balance sheets can invest counter-cyclically; those with high leverage face existential stress.

**Trough:** GDP at its cyclical low, unemployment at its cyclical high, asset prices depressed. The fear is greatest at the trough — and historically, investment at the trough has produced the highest long-term returns.

#### Leading, Lagging, and Coincident Indicators

**Leading indicators** change before the economy changes direction — they predict future conditions:
- Yield curve shape (inverted yield curve — when short-term rates exceed long-term rates — has preceded every US recession for 50 years)
- New housing starts
- Manufacturing new orders
- Consumer confidence indexes
- Stock market performance (investors price in future conditions)

**Coincident indicators** change with the economy — they confirm current conditions:
- GDP growth
- Employment levels
- Industrial production
- Personal income

**Lagging indicators** change after the economy changes direction — they confirm trends:
- Unemployment rate (employers are slow to hire and slow to fire)
- Commercial and industrial loans
- Average duration of unemployment

**The executive application:** By monitoring leading indicators, executives can anticipate economic turning points 6-18 months in advance — early enough to prepare balance sheets, adjust capital allocation, time major investments counter-cyclically, and protect the business against adverse conditions before they arrive.

### Case Study: The 2008 Global Financial Crisis — Systemic Macro Risk in Action

#### The Macro Setup

Following the 2001 recession and the September 11 attacks, the Federal Reserve cut the Federal Funds Rate aggressively — from 6.5% to 1% by 2003. At 1%, money was historically cheap. This stimulated exactly what monetary policy is designed to stimulate: borrowing and spending.

But cheap money has distributional effects. The sectors that absorb the most stimulus are those most sensitive to interest rates: housing, consumer durables, and speculative financial activity. The 2003-2006 period saw a historic housing price boom fueled by cheap mortgage credit.

#### The Financial Innovation That Amplified Risk

The housing boom was supercharged by financial innovation that separated loan origination from risk-bearing:

**The originate-to-distribute model:** Before securitization, banks originated mortgages and held them on their balance sheets. This created direct incentives for creditworthiness — the bank would lose money if borrowers defaulted. After securitization became widespread, banks originated mortgages and immediately sold them to investment banks who packaged them into securities. The originating bank no longer held the risk — the asset was someone else's problem.

**Mortgage-backed securities (MBS) and CDOs:** Investment banks packaged thousands of mortgages into securities and sold them globally. Further financial engineering created Collateralized Debt Obligations (CDOs) — securities backed by tranches of MBS. The complexity of these structures made it nearly impossible for investors to assess the underlying credit risk.

**The rating agency failure:** Credit rating agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) assigned AAA ratings to CDO tranches that, in hindsight, were composed primarily of subprime mortgage credit. The models used to assign ratings assumed that house prices were unlikely to fall simultaneously across multiple geographic markets. That assumption was false and based on historically limited data that excluded previous nationwide housing price declines.

#### The Trigger and Contagion

When the Fed began raising rates in 2004 (to 5.25% by 2006), adjustable-rate mortgages — which had made homeownership affordable at 1% rates — became unaffordable. Mortgage delinquencies began rising. Housing prices peaked in 2006 and began declining. By 2007, the housing market was clearly reversing.

The financial contagion operated through the institutional holdings of MBS and CDOs:

1. Housing prices fall → mortgage defaults rise → MBS values decline → CDO values decline
2. Financial institutions holding MBS and CDOs recognize losses → balance sheets weaken
3. Counterparty uncertainty: no one knows which institutions hold how much toxic paper → credit markets freeze
4. Liquidity crisis: firms that depended on short-term financing (the repo market, commercial paper markets) can no longer roll over their borrowing → forced asset sales → values fall further
5. The failure of Lehman Brothers in September 2008 crystallized the crisis — the "too connected to fail" assumption was shattered, and credit markets froze globally

#### Business Consequences

The macro freezing of credit markets transmitted to the real economy with speed and severity that most executives had never experienced:

**Working capital crisis:** Many large companies (including non-financial companies like General Electric, major retailers, and manufacturers) relied on commercial paper markets for short-term financing of working capital. When those markets froze, companies couldn't fund operations.

**Recession depth:** GDP fell sharply; unemployment rose from 5% to 10% by 2009. Consumer spending collapsed. Industry-specific demand fell 20-50% in automotive, housing, commercial real estate, and consumer discretionary categories.

**Credit tightening:** Banks dramatically reduced lending to businesses and consumers, further contracting demand and creating a vicious cycle.

**Balance sheet strength determined survival:** Companies that entered the crisis with strong balance sheets — low debt, significant cash reserves, stable cash flows — survived and often gained market share. Companies with high leverage faced existential stress, regardless of their underlying business quality.

#### The Macroeconomic Lessons for Executives

**Lesson 1: Systemic risk is not priced correctly by markets**
Before the crisis, financial markets were pricing relatively low risk across most asset classes. The actual systemic risk — the degree to which interconnected institutions might amplify a localized housing correction into a global financial crisis — was severely underestimated. When systemic events occur, historically unprecedented things happen.

**Lesson 2: Balance sheet strength is a strategic asset**
Apple, for example, entered the crisis with enormous cash reserves. During the crisis, the company continued to invest in product development, customer relationships, and supplier partnerships that distressed competitors could not afford to maintain. When conditions improved, Apple's advantage was structural.

**Lesson 3: Macro analysis must be embedded in strategic planning**
The companies that performed best through 2008-2010 had, in many cases, anticipated deteriorating conditions and built resilience into their balance sheets, supply chains, and operating models before the crisis arrived. Macro awareness is a precondition for that kind of proactive positioning.

**Lesson 4: The policy response reshaped the competitive environment**
The Federal Reserve's aggressive response — cutting rates to zero, launching QE — created a decade of ultra-low rates that profoundly shaped industry economics. Companies that understood this policy trajectory and made appropriate capital allocation decisions (acquiring at depressed valuations in 2009, investing in capacity at low interest rates) captured enormous value.

### Executive Takeaway

Macroeconomics is not the exclusive domain of economists. It is the environmental context within which every strategic decision operates. The executive who understands what the yield curve signals about future recession probability, what Fed rate decisions imply for their cost of capital, how dollar appreciation affects their international competitive position, and how the business cycle should influence their capital allocation timing — that executive has a profound strategic advantage.

You cannot control the macroeconomic environment. But with sufficient understanding of how it works, you can position your organization to be resilient when adversity arrives and opportunistic when opportunity appears.

The most common executive error is treating macroeconomic risk as unforeseeable and therefore unmanageable. In reality, most major macroeconomic events — recessions, inflation cycles, rate cycles, currency movements — develop over months or years, with leading indicators that provide meaningful warning to those who know where to look.
