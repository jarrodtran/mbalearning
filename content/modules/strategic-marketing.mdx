# MODULE 5:STRATEGIC MARKETING & POSITIONING
## Inspired by Kellogg School of Management

### Executive Briefing (SCQA)

**Situation:** Marketing is the most misunderstood function in business. At the executive level, it is almost never about advertising. It is about the fundamental question of who your customer is, what they need, why they should choose you, and how you create and capture value from that choice.

**Complication:** Most companies have a marketing function that produces campaigns rather than strategy. They know their product features but not their customers' jobs. They know their market share but not why customers choose them or switch to competitors. And they have no coherent answer to the most important strategic marketing question: what do we stand for, and for whom?

**Question:** How do executives develop a rigorous marketing strategy that begins with customer understanding, uses that understanding to make precise targeting decisions, builds a positioning that creates genuine competitive differentiation, and deploys a marketing mix that efficiently converts strategy into growth?

**Answer:** Through the STP framework (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning) combined with Value Proposition Design, a disciplined 4Ps marketing mix, and a performance measurement system that connects marketing investment to business outcomes.

### Core Framework 1: Market Segmentation

Segmentation is the process of dividing a heterogeneous market into groups of customers who share similar needs, behaviors, or characteristics — and who will therefore respond similarly to a given marketing strategy.

The purpose of segmentation is not academic categorization. It is to identify groups of customers who can be served profitably with a differentiated offering. A good segmentation reveals where the money is and who you can win.

#### Segmentation Bases

**Geographic segmentation:** Dividing by location — country, region, city, climate. Relevant when customer needs, preferences, or competitive conditions vary by geography. A winter clothing brand segments differently in Minnesota vs. Florida.

**Demographic segmentation:** Dividing by age, gender, income, education, occupation, family status. The most commonly used and often the most overused. Demographics are proxies for needs. They work when demographics reliably predict behavior; they fail when they don't.

**Psychographic segmentation:** Dividing by values, personality, lifestyle, and attitudes. More predictive than demographics in many categories because it captures why people buy, not just who they are. REI's customer is not defined by age or income but by a lifestyle orientation (outdoor adventure, environmental consciousness).

**Behavioral segmentation:** Dividing by purchase behavior — frequency, occasion, loyalty status, usage rate, benefits sought. Often the most actionable segmentation because it is directly tied to the purchase decision. Airlines segment by frequency (business travelers fly constantly, leisure travelers fly occasionally) because frequency determines both profitability and sensitivity to different aspects of the value proposition.

**Needs-based segmentation:** Dividing by the specific job the customer is trying to accomplish. The most strategically powerful segmentation because it connects directly to the JTBD framework. What problem are they trying to solve? What outcome are they trying to achieve?

#### What Makes a Good Segment?

A good segment passes five tests:

1. **Measurable** — You can quantify the size and purchasing power of the segment
2. **Substantial** — The segment is large enough to be profitable to serve
3. **Accessible** — You can reach and serve the segment through your marketing and distribution channels
4. **Differentiable** — The segment responds differently from other segments to different marketing strategies
5. **Actionable** — You can design effective programs to attract and serve the segment

### Core Framework 2: Targeting

Targeting is choosing which segment (or segments) to serve. This is a strategic resource allocation decision — you are deciding where to concentrate your competitive effort.

#### Targeting Strategies

**Undifferentiated (mass) marketing:** Treat the entire market as a single segment. Design one product and one marketing strategy for everyone. Only viable when customer needs are genuinely homogeneous (commodity products) or when the company has such strong cost advantages that standardization pays for itself. Coca-Cola's core product is close to undifferentiated.

**Differentiated marketing:** Serve multiple segments with customized products and strategies for each. Higher cost, but potentially higher revenue per segment as the offering better matches each group's needs. Marriott serves budget travelers (Fairfield), business travelers (Courtyard), upscale travelers (Marriott), luxury travelers (Ritz-Carlton), and extended-stay travelers (Residence Inn) with distinct brands, products, and marketing programs.

**Concentrated (niche) marketing:** Focus all resources on one segment. Accept the limited total addressable market in exchange for deep competitive position. Porsche (performance cars), Whole Foods (premium natural foods), Bloomberg (financial professionals). This is the "focus" generic strategy applied to the marketing function.

**Micromarketing:** Customizing products and marketing to individual customers or very small local groups. Enabled by data and technology. Amazon's product recommendations, Netflix's content suggestions, and Spotify's Discover Weekly playlist are all forms of micromarketing at scale.

#### Segment Attractiveness Evaluation

Before selecting a target, evaluate each segment on:

1. **Segment size** — Is it large enough to support the business?
2. **Growth rate** — Is it growing or shrinking?
3. **Competitive intensity** — How many strong competitors are already serving this segment?
4. **Company fit** — Does the segment play to the company's strengths?
5. **Profitability potential** — Given pricing power and cost to serve, can you earn adequate margins?

### Core Framework 3: Positioning

Positioning is the strategic decision about how you want your brand to be perceived in the mind of your target customer — relative to the alternatives they consider.

Al Ries and Jack Trout, in *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* (1981), made the foundational observation: positioning is not about what you do to a product. It is about what you do to the mind of a prospect.

The customer's mind is crowded. Thousands of brands compete for limited mental real estate. A brand that occupies a clear, distinctive, relevant position in the customer's mind has an advantage that is difficult to dislodge.

#### The Positioning Statement

The positioning statement is a concise articulation of the strategic choices you've made. It is an internal document — a strategic anchor for all marketing decisions — not a tagline or an advertisement.

**Format:**
"For [target customer], [brand] is the [frame of reference] that delivers [point of difference] because [reason to believe]."

**Example (Apple iPhone, early days):**
"For people who want the most intuitive and beautiful mobile computing experience, iPhone is the smartphone that puts the full power of a computer in your pocket because Apple uniquely designs both the hardware and software with relentless attention to user experience."

**The four components:**
- **Frame of reference:** What category or alternative are you being compared to? This tells the customer how to think about you.
- **Target customer:** Who specifically are you positioning for? Not everyone.
- **Point of difference:** What do you deliver that competitors don't — and that your target customer values?
- **Reason to believe:** Why should the customer believe your claim?

#### The Positioning Map

A positioning map visualizes the competitive landscape on two dimensions that matter most to the target customer. It reveals white space (where customer needs are underserved) and crowded space (where competition is intense).

**Classic example — Automotive positioning map:**
- X-axis: Price (economy to luxury)
- Y-axis: Brand character (conservative to sporty)
- Insight: The luxury/sporty quadrant (BMW, Mercedes AMG) is crowded. The economy/sporty quadrant (VW GTI, Mazda) is underserved relative to demand.

### Core Framework 4: Value Proposition Design

A value proposition is the full package of benefits that a company promises to deliver to customers. It is the reason someone should buy from you rather than from a competitor.

Alexander Osterwalder's Value Proposition Canvas connects two sides:

**Customer Profile (what customers want):**
- **Jobs:** What tasks is the customer trying to accomplish? What problems are they trying to solve? What needs are they trying to satisfy?
- **Pains:** What annoys, frustrates, or risks failure for the customer? What obstacles prevent them from getting their job done?
- **Gains:** What outcomes does the customer want? What would delight them beyond their expectations?

**Value Map (what you offer):**
- **Products and Services:** What specifically are you offering?
- **Pain Relievers:** How do your products and services eliminate or reduce customer pains?
- **Gain Creators:** How do your products and services create the outcomes customers want?

**Fit:** A strong value proposition achieves fit — every significant customer pain has a corresponding pain reliever, and every significant customer gain has a corresponding gain creator.

### Core Framework 5: The 4Ps Marketing Mix

The marketing mix (4Ps) is the tactical implementation of the positioning strategy. The 4Ps must be internally consistent — they must all reinforce the same positioning.

#### Product

What are you actually selling? This includes:
- Core benefit (the fundamental job the product does)
- Actual product (features, design, quality, brand name, packaging)
- Augmented product (installation, after-sale service, warranty, delivery)

**Strategic questions:** What features are essential vs. peripheral? What can be removed without diminishing value? What can be added that customers would pay for? How does the product need to evolve to stay relevant?

#### Price

What do you charge, and what does that price communicate?

Price is not just a revenue variable. It is a positioning signal. A luxury brand that drops its price is not just capturing more customers — it is changing its positioning. A professional services firm that charges higher fees is not just extracting more value — it is communicating confidence and quality.

**Pricing strategies:**
- **Cost-plus:** Add a markup to the cost of production. Simple, but ignores customer value and competitive dynamics.
- **Value-based:** Price based on the value delivered to the customer. Requires deep understanding of customer economics.
- **Competitive:** Price relative to competitors. Appropriate when products are relatively undifferentiated.
- **Penetration:** Enter with a low price to build market share, then raise. Netflix's early strategy.
- **Skimming:** Enter with a high price to capture maximum value from early adopters, then reduce. Apple's iPhone strategy.

#### Place (Distribution)

How do customers access your product?

Distribution decisions determine who can buy your product and at what friction cost. The right distribution strategy depends on the product, the customer, and the positioning:

- **Direct (DTC):** Company sells directly to customers. Higher margins, direct customer relationship, more control over experience. Tesla's direct sales model.
- **Retail:** Sell through third-party stores. Greater reach, lower margins, less control.
- **Online:** E-commerce enables global reach with low overhead. Requires traffic acquisition.
- **B2B channels:** Distributors, resellers, value-added resellers. Appropriate for complex products requiring local expertise.

#### Promotion

How do you communicate your value proposition?

The promotion mix includes:
- **Advertising:** Paid media (TV, digital, out-of-home, print). Builds awareness at scale.
- **Public relations:** Earned media. More credible than advertising; harder to control.
- **Content marketing:** Creating valuable content that attracts and engages the target audience. HubSpot's entire growth model was built on content marketing.
- **Social media:** Direct communication with customers and communities. High engagement potential; requires authentic voice.
- **Sales promotion:** Short-term incentives (discounts, coupons, contests). Drives immediate purchase; can damage brand if overused.
- **Direct marketing:** Targeted communications to specific individuals (email, direct mail, telemarketing).
- **Personal selling:** Direct sales force. High cost per contact but essential for complex, high-value products.

### Case Study: Apple's "Think Different" — Positioning as Strategy

#### The Setup

In 1997, Apple was twelve weeks from bankruptcy. Gil Amelio had been fired. Steve Jobs had returned. The product line was a mess — dozens of products, no coherent strategy, declining market share. Apple needed more than a new product. It needed a new identity.

Jobs hired TBWA\Chiat\Day and gave them a brief that was unusual for a struggling company: don't tell me what Apple makes. Tell me what Apple stands for.

The resulting campaign — "Think Different" — did not show a single Apple product. It showed Albert Einstein, Martin Luther King Jr., Mahatma Gandhi, Amelia Earhart, Bob Dylan, and other historical figures who had changed the world by seeing it differently.

#### The Positioning Strategy

**Target customer:** Creative professionals and aspiring creatives who felt that computers were for business people and engineers — not for them. People who identified with counterculture, creativity, and rebellion against the status quo.

**Frame of reference:** Not "personal computer" — Apple positioned against the generic category. The frame of reference was "tool for people who want to change the world."

**Point of difference:** Apple products are made for people who think differently — for the rebels, the misfits, the round pegs in the square holes.

**Reason to believe:** Apple's design philosophy, its history (the original Mac was literally designed to make computing human), and Jobs himself as a living embodiment of the positioning.

**The result:** Apple's market share was not immediately transformed by the campaign. But the brand positioning anchored every product decision, every design decision, and every marketing decision for the next two decades. The iMac, iPod, iPhone, and iPad all reinforced the same positioning. "Think Different" was not a campaign — it was a strategy.

#### The Marketing Lesson

Apple's case demonstrates that positioning is upstream of product. The positioning decision — what do we stand for, for whom — should drive product decisions, not follow them. Apple didn't make products and then figure out how to market them. They decided what they stood for and then made products that embodied that identity.
