# MODULE 9:GLOBAL STRATEGY & EXPANSION
## Inspired by INSEAD

### Executive Briefing (SCQA)

**Situation:** The world's most valuable markets are increasingly outside any single company's home country. The United States represents approximately 25% of global GDP. The fastest-growing consumer markets are in Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Companies that limit themselves to domestic competition are leaving the majority of the world's opportunity untouched.

**Complication:** International expansion is not simply domestic strategy applied to a new geography. Every market has its own competitive dynamics, regulatory environment, cultural context, and consumer behavior patterns. Companies that assume their domestic success formula translates directly to international markets consistently underperform — and sometimes catastrophically fail.

**Question:** How do executives evaluate international expansion opportunities rigorously, select the most attractive markets, choose the appropriate entry mode, and adapt their business model to the requirements of each market while maintaining strategic coherence?

**Answer:** By applying the Ansoff Matrix to frame growth options, the CAGE Distance framework to evaluate market attractiveness, a disciplined entry mode selection process, and a cultural intelligence framework that prevents the assumption of universality.

### Core Framework 1: Ansoff Matrix — Growth Strategy Options

Igor Ansoff's growth matrix (1957) provides a simple but powerful framework for categorizing growth strategies along two dimensions: products (existing vs. new) and markets (existing vs. new).

**Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets)**
Grow by selling more of what you already sell to customers you already serve. The lowest risk growth strategy because you're leveraging existing capabilities and relationships.

*Levers:* Increase purchase frequency, expand customer wallet share, win customers from competitors, reduce customer churn.

*Example:* Amazon encouraging existing Prime members to use more Amazon services (AWS, Kindle, music streaming). The customer is already acquired; the marginal cost of incremental services is low.

**Quadrant 2: Market Development (existing products, new markets)**
Grow by taking existing products into new customer segments or geographies. Moderate risk — you understand the product but not the new market.

*Example:* Starbucks entering China with its existing coffee shop format. The product (coffee, café experience) was familiar; the market (Chinese consumers, Chinese regulatory environment, Chinese real estate) was new.

**Quadrant 3: Product Development (new products, existing markets)**
Grow by developing new products for existing customers. Moderate risk — you understand the customer but must develop new capabilities.

*Example:* Apple introducing iPhone to its existing Mac and iPod customer base. Apple had deep relationships with its customers and understood their preferences; the smartphone represented new product development built on existing brand equity and customer trust.

**Quadrant 4: Diversification (new products, new markets)**
Grow by entering new markets with new products. Highest risk because you have no existing advantage in either dimension.

*Related diversification:* Entering markets with some connection to your existing business (Amazon entering cloud computing — new product, new market, but leveraging existing infrastructure and technical capabilities)

*Unrelated diversification:* Entering markets with no connection to your existing business. Generally value-destroying at the corporate level (conglomerate discount).

### Core Framework 2: CAGE Distance Framework

Pankaj Ghemawat developed the CAGE framework to help executives evaluate the "distance" between their home market and a potential new market. Greater distance means greater difficulty and cost of doing business.

**C — Cultural Distance**
Differences in language, ethnicity, religion, social norms, values, and attitudes. Cultural distance affects how customers perceive products, how employees respond to management practices, and how relationships between companies are structured.

*High cultural distance example:* An American consumer products company entering Saudi Arabia faces significant cultural distance — religious practices affect product formulation (alcohol, pork-based ingredients), gender norms affect marketing and retail practices, and family structures affect purchasing decisions.

*Low cultural distance example:* A US company entering Canada or the UK faces relatively low cultural distance — shared language (largely), similar legal traditions, similar consumer behavior patterns.

**A — Administrative Distance**
Differences in legal systems, regulatory environments, governance quality, colonial history, and institutional frameworks. Administrative distance affects how businesses operate, how contracts are enforced, and how intellectual property is protected.

*High administrative distance example:* A technology company entering a market with weak intellectual property protection faces the risk of product copying without legal recourse. A company entering a market with significant state ownership faces competition from enterprises with different profit motives and access to government support.

**G — Geographic Distance**
Physical distance between markets. Affects transportation costs, supply chain complexity, communication challenges (time zones), and the cost of deploying management talent.

*Relevant factors beyond pure distance:* Whether the country is landlocked (affects transport costs), climate differences (affects product requirements), and infrastructure quality (affects distribution).

**E — Economic Distance**
Differences in income levels, wealth distribution, consumer purchasing power, factor costs (labor, land, capital), and economic development stage.

*High economic distance example:* A luxury goods brand entering sub-Saharan Africa with a product priced for European consumers faces extreme economic distance. A small percentage of the population can afford the product; distribution to reach that segment is expensive.

*Strategic implication of economic distance:* Companies often need to redesign their products or business models to compete in markets with significantly different economic profiles. P&G's "bottom of the pyramid" strategies — single-use sachets of shampoo and detergent in India — are an adaptation to economic distance.

### Core Framework 3: Entry Mode Selection

How you enter a market is as important as whether you enter it. Entry mode determines your control over operations, your capital commitment, your speed of entry, and your exposure to risk.

**Mode 1: Exporting**
Produce in the home country, sell in the target market.

*Advantages:* Low capital commitment, low risk, rapid entry, no need to establish local operations.
*Disadvantages:* High transportation costs, potential tariffs, limited ability to customize for local market, no local presence or relationships.
*Best for:* Testing market attractiveness before committing capital; products with low transportation costs; markets with high tariffs on manufacturing but not products.

**Mode 2: Licensing and Franchising**
License your technology, brand, or business model to a local partner in exchange for royalties.

*Advantages:* Very low capital commitment, no operational complexity, rapid scaling, local partner brings market knowledge.
*Disadvantages:* Loss of control over quality and brand experience, risk of creating a future competitor, royalties are a small fraction of the value created.
*Best for:* Markets where the cost of establishing owned operations is prohibitive; markets where local knowledge is the primary success factor; businesses where replication is the primary value driver (restaurants, retail).

**Mode 3: Joint Venture**
Establish a jointly owned entity with a local partner.

*Advantages:* Shared capital risk, access to local partner's capabilities and relationships, may be required by local regulations (China required JVs in many industries).
*Disadvantages:* Governance complexity, potential partner conflict, profit sharing.
*Best for:* Markets where local relationships are essential; markets with regulatory requirements for local ownership; markets where the partner contributes genuinely scarce capabilities.

**Mode 4: Wholly Owned Subsidiary (Greenfield)**
Establish a new, fully owned operation in the target market from scratch.

*Advantages:* Full control over operations, culture, and strategy; no profit sharing; ability to protect proprietary technology.
*Disadvantages:* High capital commitment, slow entry, no local knowledge or relationships, full operational complexity.
*Best for:* Markets where operational control is critical (high-tech manufacturing, services requiring specific quality standards); markets where the entry will be long-term and large-scale.

**Mode 5: Acquisition**
Acquire an existing local company.

*Advantages:* Immediate market presence, established brand and customer relationships, existing talent, rapid entry.
*Disadvantages:* Acquisition premium, integration risk, potential for cultural conflict between acquirer and target, risk of overpaying.
*Best for:* Markets where speed matters; markets where an existing player has a position that would take years to build organically; companies where the acquisition price is justified by the strategic value of the immediate position.

### Core Framework 4: Cultural Intelligence in Global Business

Geert Hofstede's cultural dimensions framework provides a systematic way to understand cultural differences that affect business practices.

**Power Distance:** The degree to which less powerful members of a society accept and expect that power is distributed unequally.

*High power distance cultures (Malaysia, Mexico):* Hierarchical organizations, deference to authority, top-down decision making. Western leaders who try to implement flat, consensus-based management in these cultures often create confusion and underperformance.

*Low power distance cultures (Denmark, Netherlands):* Flat organizations, consultation before decisions, employees expect to have input. Leaders who behave autocratically in these cultures destroy morale and talent.

**Individualism vs. Collectivism:** The degree to which individuals are integrated into groups.

*Individualist cultures (US, UK, Australia):* Emphasis on individual achievement and rights. Marketing focused on personal benefit, performance management focused on individual contribution.

*Collectivist cultures (China, Japan, much of Latin America):* Group harmony and loyalty are primary values. Marketing focused on family or social benefit, performance management must account for team cohesion.

**Uncertainty Avoidance:** The degree to which members of a culture feel uncomfortable with uncertainty and ambiguity.

*High uncertainty avoidance (Japan, Germany):* Preference for rules, procedures, and structure. Companies operating in these markets need more formal processes and clearer communications.

*Low uncertainty avoidance (Singapore, Jamaica):* More comfortable with ambiguity and change. More entrepreneurial, less process-dependent.

**Long-term vs. Short-term Orientation:**

*Long-term oriented cultures (China, Japan):* Emphasis on future rewards, persistence, thrift, and adaptation. Business relationships are invested in over years. Quick wins matter less than long-term positioning.

*Short-term oriented cultures (US, Australia):* Emphasis on present results, quick returns. Business decisions are evaluated on current-period metrics.

### Case Study: Starbucks' China Expansion — Local Adaptation at Scale

#### The Setup

Starbucks entered China in 1999 with a store in Beijing. At the time, the conventional wisdom said China was a tea culture — coffee was foreign, unfamiliar, and expensive relative to local incomes. Starbucks ignored the conventional wisdom.

By 2023, China was Starbucks' second-largest market, with over 6,000 stores. The company's China strategy is one of the most studied international expansion case studies of the past two decades.

#### The Distance Analysis

**Cultural distance:** Significant. China has a millennia-old tea culture. Coffee was associated with Western culture — initially a barrier, ultimately a positioning opportunity. Starbucks leaned into the Western association as a status signal for Chinese urban professionals.

**Administrative distance:** High. China requires foreign companies in many sectors to operate through joint ventures. Starbucks initially entered through a licensed partnership, then acquired full ownership as regulations evolved. The regulatory environment required constant navigation.

**Geographic distance:** Extreme. China is vast — Starbucks had to build a supply chain, logistics network, and store development capability across a continent-sized country.

**Economic distance:** Evolving. In 1999, China's consumer class was small. By the 2010s, the urban middle class had grown to hundreds of millions, dramatically expanding Starbucks' addressable market.

#### The Strategic Adaptations

**Premium positioning:** Rather than competing on price (which would have been impossible given China's cost structure and local competitors), Starbucks positioned as a premium experience — a "third place" for China's rising urban professionals. The coffee price point was aspirational. The store experience (large, elegant stores in prime locations) reinforced status.

**Store design adaptation:** Chinese Starbucks stores are, on average, significantly larger than their US counterparts. They feature local design elements and artwork, more communal seating (reflecting collectivist cultural preferences), and layouts that encourage lingering — critical in a culture where the "third place" value proposition resonated.

**Product localization:** Starbucks adapted its menu to Chinese preferences — tea-based beverages, mooncake collaborations, red bean and taro flavors. The core coffee menu remained but was supplemented with locally relevant options.

**Digital leadership:** Starbucks China became a digital innovation leader for the global company. Mobile ordering, delivery partnerships (with Alibaba/ele.me), and gamified loyalty programs were piloted in China before rollout elsewhere.

#### The Lesson

Starbucks succeeded in China not by replicating its US model but by understanding what was transferable (the premium positioning, the "third place" concept, the quality commitment) and what needed local adaptation (store design, menu, digital infrastructure, pricing). The CAGE analysis correctly predicted the dimensions of distance; the strategic response was to lean into some dimensions (cultural distance as a feature, not a bug — Western association added status) while actively bridging others (product adaptation, local hiring).
