# MODULE 3:INNOVATION & MARKET DISRUPTION
## Inspired by Clayton Christensen, Harvard Business School

### Executive Briefing (SCQA)

**Situation:** Every successful company faces a paradox. The better they serve their best customers — investing in higher-quality products, premium features, and superior service — the more vulnerable they become to a specific kind of competitive attack that they are structurally unable to see coming.

**Complication:** Clayton Christensen called this the Innovator's Dilemma. Established companies, following rational management practices and listening carefully to their best customers, consistently fail to respond to disruptive innovations. Not because they are incompetent. But because disruption enters at the bottom of the market, serving customers that incumbents don't value, with products that incumbents don't respect — until it is too late.

**Question:** How do executives distinguish between sustaining innovation (which makes their current business better) and disruptive innovation (which threatens to make their business obsolete)? And how do they develop strategies to either defend against disruption or use disruption as a weapon?

**Answer:** By mastering the Jobs-to-be-Done framework, understanding the mechanics of low-end and new-market disruption, and building the organizational capability to manage two business models simultaneously — the existing business that pays the bills and the emerging business that will eventually replace it.

---

### Core Framework 1: Sustaining vs. Disruptive Innovation

Christensen's most important contribution is the distinction between two types of innovation that look similar on the surface but have fundamentally different strategic implications.

#### Sustaining Innovation

Sustaining innovations make existing products better for existing customers. They improve performance on dimensions that mainstream customers already value.

Examples:
- Intel releasing a faster processor for existing PC categories
- Toyota improving fuel efficiency in existing vehicle models
- Netflix improving its recommendation algorithm for existing subscribers
- A hospital adding a new wing to serve more patients with the same care model

Sustaining innovation is important and necessary. But it does not create new markets. It does not disrupt incumbents. And — critically — incumbents are very good at it. Large companies with strong R&D capabilities, established customer relationships, and clear performance trajectories consistently win sustaining innovation battles against smaller challengers.

#### Disruptive Innovation

Disruptive innovations do not start by making existing products better for existing customers. They start by making a simpler, cheaper, more accessible version of a product available to customers who previously couldn't afford or access the mainstream product.

The disruption pattern works in two stages:

**Stage 1:** The disruptor enters at the bottom of the market (or creates an entirely new market) with a product that is "worse" on the dimensions incumbents care about but "good enough" for a different set of customers. Incumbents are rationally uninterested — these customers are low-margin and the product is inferior.

**Stage 2:** The disruptor improves. The initially simple product gets better, faster, and cheaper. It eventually crosses the "good enough" threshold for mainstream customers. By this point, the disruptor has built scale, refined their model, and established customer relationships. Incumbents, focused on serving their premium customers, wake up to find the market underneath them has been hollowed out.

#### The Disruption Trajectory

Christensen observed that technology improvement consistently follows a faster trajectory than customer needs improvement. In other words: products always get better faster than customers need them to get better.

This creates the disruption window. When products exceed customer needs on mainstream performance dimensions, customers become less willing to pay for further improvement. They become vulnerable to a product that is "good enough" on performance but dramatically better on price, convenience, or accessibility.

### Core Framework 2: Low-End Disruption vs. New-Market Disruption

Christensen identified two distinct disruption patterns:

#### Low-End Disruption

Low-end disruptors enter at the bottom of an existing market, targeting customers whom incumbents are "overserving" — providing more performance than those customers need or are willing to pay for.

**Mechanics:**
1. Incumbents focus resources on their most profitable, most demanding customers (usually at the high end of the market)
2. Low-end disruptors enter with a "good enough" product at a much lower price
3. Incumbents don't respond because the low-end customers are low-margin and the product is inferior
4. Disruptors improve and move upmarket, taking more and more customers
5. By the time incumbents recognize the threat, the disruptor has scale, cost advantages, and customer relationships

**Classic example:** Steel minimills vs. integrated steel mills
Minimills (Nucor was the pioneer) entered the steel industry in the 1960s and 1970s making low-grade rebar from scrap metal. Integrated steel mills (U.S. Steel, Bethlehem) were delighted to exit the rebar business — it was their least profitable product. Minimills kept improving. They eventually learned to make better grades of steel. Today, minimills produce most U.S. steel and every major integrated mill has either failed or dramatically shrunk.

**Classic example:** Southwest Airlines disrupting legacy carriers
Southwest entered with a simpler product (no meals, no assigned seats, secondary airports) targeting price-sensitive customers whom legacy carriers served unprofitably. Legacy carriers were happy to let Southwest have those customers. Southwest then improved and moved into more routes. Today Southwest is the largest domestic carrier by passenger count.

#### New-Market Disruption

New-market disruptors don't take customers from incumbents — they create new customers by making a product accessible to people who previously couldn't afford it or lacked the skill to use it.

**Mechanics:**
1. Incumbents serve only those customers with the money or skill to use the mainstream product
2. Disruptors create a simpler, cheaper, more accessible version that expands the market to non-consumers
3. Incumbents don't respond because the disruptor isn't taking their customers
4. The new market grows larger than the incumbent's original market

**Classic example:** Personal computers disrupting mainframes and minicomputers
Mainframe computers required specialized operators and cost millions. Minicomputers were cheaper but still required expertise. Personal computers put computing in the hands of non-experts — students, small business owners, hobbyists. IBM and DEC initially dismissed PCs as toys. The toy became the standard platform for business computing.

**Classic example:** Airbnb disrupting hotels
Airbnb didn't initially take customers from Marriott or Hilton. It created a new market — people who would travel to a city but couldn't afford or didn't want a traditional hotel. Price-sensitive travelers, people visiting for extended periods, travelers wanting a "local" experience. Marriott initially dismissed Airbnb because it wasn't competing for Marriott's customers. Eventually, Airbnb moved upmarket into nicer properties, and the disruption became undeniable.

### Core Framework 3: Jobs-to-be-Done

The Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) theory, which Christensen developed alongside Bob Moesta and others, reframes how we think about customer needs. The core insight: customers don't buy products. They "hire" products to do a job for them.

**The milkshake example (Christensen's most famous illustration):**
A fast food chain wanted to improve milkshake sales. Traditional market research segmented customers by demographics (age, gender) and psychographics (active lifestyle, family-oriented). The research yielded nothing actionable.

A researcher instead asked: what job is the customer hiring the milkshake to do? He discovered that most milkshakes were purchased in the morning by commuters who wanted something to eat while driving that would keep them full until lunch, was easy to consume with one hand, and took a long time to finish (so they had something to do during the commute). The milkshake was competing not with other milkshakes but with bananas, bagels, and breakfast sandwiches — all of which were inferior to the milkshake for this particular job.

A completely different customer hired milkshakes in the afternoon: parents buying them for children as a treat after school. This customer wanted a smaller, less filling milkshake — a different job entirely.

**The strategic implication:** If you define your competition by product category (milkshake vs. milkshake), you miss the real competitive landscape. If you define it by the job the customer is trying to do (portable, long-lasting, one-handed breakfast that requires no preparation), you see the real competitive set and can design superior solutions.

#### JTBD Analysis Framework

For any product or market, apply the JTBD lens:

**Functional dimension:** What practical task is the customer trying to accomplish? (Get to my destination, communicate with my team, store my data)

**Emotional dimension:** How does the customer want to feel while accomplishing it? (Confident, safe, in control, sophisticated, like a good parent)

**Social dimension:** How does the customer want to be perceived by others? (Successful, environmentally conscious, a good provider, an early adopter)

**The disruptive opportunity:** When a product is over-engineered on functional dimensions but under-delivering on emotional or social dimensions, there is a disruption opportunity for a simpler product that nails the emotional and social jobs while being "good enough" on function.

### Core Framework 4: The Incumbent's Dilemma — Why Good Management Fails

Christensen's most provocative argument is that the companies that fail to respond to disruption are often the best-managed companies in their industries. They fail not despite good management but because of it.

The logic:

1. **Resource allocation processes favor the core business.** In a well-managed company, resources flow to initiatives with the best near-term financial returns. Disruptive businesses are initially small, low-margin, and uncertain. They consistently lose internal resource allocation battles to the core business.

2. **Customer feedback loops drive toward sustaining innovation.** Well-managed companies listen carefully to their best customers. Their best customers want better versions of existing products — sustaining innovation. Disruptive innovations serve customers the company doesn't currently have or doesn't value. The feedback loop systematically steers the company away from disruption.

3. **Values and cost structures favor the high end.** Companies develop cost structures, incentive systems, and cultural values calibrated to their core business. These are all wrong for a disruptive new business. The overhead structure that supports a premium business makes it impossible to profitably serve a low-end market.

4. **Strategic planning validates the core.** Forecasting tools, market research methods, and financial models are all calibrated to the existing business. They systematically underestimate the potential of disruptive markets (which are initially small) and overestimate the resilience of the core business (which feels solid until it suddenly isn't).

### Core Framework 5: Disruption Defense and Offense

#### Defense — How incumbents can respond to disruption

**Option 1: Create a separate organizational unit**
The only reliable way to fight disruption is to create a separate business unit with its own P&L, its own leadership, its own resource allocation process, and its own cost structure. The disruptive business must be insulated from the core business's values, processes, and customer relationships.

*Example:* Amazon's approach to new business lines. AWS was developed as a separate organizational unit with a separate P&L and separate leadership. It was not forced to meet the same margin requirements as Amazon's e-commerce business. Today AWS generates most of Amazon's operating profit.

*Failure example:* Clayton Christensen's analysis of disk drive manufacturers who failed to respond to disruptive smaller form factor drives, even when their engineering teams developed them, because the new drives competed with their existing products and were always given lower resource priority.

**Option 2: Acquire the disruptor**
Buy the disruptive company while it is still small enough to be affordable. The risk is integration — many acquisitions of innovative companies destroy the very thing that made the company valuable.

*Success example:* Facebook's acquisition of Instagram in 2012 for $1 billion (before Instagram had significant revenue). Instagram was beginning to disrupt Facebook's photo-sharing functionality. The acquisition neutralized the threat and Facebook has extracted enormous value.

*Failure example:* Daimler's acquisition of Chrysler. A different kind of failure — acquiring a company without understanding how to preserve what made it valuable.

**Option 3: Accept disruption of the low end and move upmarket**
Cede the low end of the market to the disruptor and deliberately move upmarket to a position with higher margins and more demanding customers. This is a viable strategy if the high end of the market is large enough to sustain the business.

*Example:* Intel has repeatedly ceded the low end of the processor market to competitors (ARM, AMD at various points) while investing in higher-performance processors for data centers and professional use.

#### Offense — How challengers use disruption as a weapon

**Step 1:** Identify overserved customers — customers in the incumbent's market who are paying for performance they don't need or can't use.

**Step 2:** Identify non-consumers — people who would benefit from the product but can't afford it or lack the skill to use it.

**Step 3:** Design a "good enough" product that serves either the overserved customers or the non-consumers at dramatically lower cost or with dramatically greater accessibility.

**Step 4:** Start in a market segment that the incumbent cannot profitably defend, build scale, and improve.

**Step 5:** Move upmarket as the product improves, taking more and more of the incumbent's customers.

### Case Study: Kodak and the Digital Photography Disruption

#### The Setup

Kodak was one of the most innovative and profitable companies in American history. For most of the 20th century, Kodak dominated the photographic film industry, earning extraordinary margins on consumables (film, developing chemicals, photographic paper). The company's brand was synonymous with memory, family, and photography.

Here is the extraordinary irony: Kodak invented the digital camera. In 1975, Steve Sasson, an engineer at Kodak, built the first digital camera prototype. He showed it to Kodak's leadership. Their response was to shelve it. A digital camera would eliminate the need for film. Film was the business.

#### The Disruption Trajectory

Digital photography followed the classic disruption pattern with precision:

**1975-1990:** Digital cameras are expensive, low-resolution, and impractical. Kodak's leadership correctly observes that digital photography is inferior to film. They invest in sustaining innovation for their film business.

**1990-2000:** Digital cameras improve rapidly. Resolution increases. Prices fall. But they are still clearly inferior to professional film photography. Kodak watches but continues to invest primarily in film.

**2000-2005:** Digital cameras cross the "good enough" threshold for the mass consumer market. Resolution sufficient for family photos, prices accessible to middle-class consumers, no developing required. Film sales begin declining. Kodak launches digital cameras but lacks the software ecosystem (photo sharing, printing) that digital photography enables.

**2005-2012:** Smartphones integrate cameras. The smartphone camera disrupts the digital camera market. Now even dedicated digital cameras face disruption. Film is functionally dead for consumer photography.

Kodak filed for bankruptcy in 2012.

#### The Three Critical Errors

**Error 1: Defining the business as "film" rather than "capturing memories"**
If Kodak had defined itself as being in the memory capture business, digital photography would have been a strategic opportunity rather than a threat. But the company's identity, cost structure, and profit model were all built around film as the medium. Digital wasn't a better version of their product — it was the elimination of their product.

**Error 2: Managing digital photography as a sustaining innovation**
When Kodak did pursue digital photography, it treated it as an extension of the film business — a sustaining innovation rather than a disruptive one. The digital business was managed within the existing organizational structure, with the same overhead requirements and margin expectations. It could never compete with pure-play digital companies operating with different cost structures and different business models.

**Error 3: Failing to see the non-consumer opportunity**
Digital photography, particularly when integrated into smartphones, created an enormous market of non-consumers — people who weren't buying film cameras or professional cameras but would capture and share images if the barrier (cost, skill, developing process) was removed. Instagram, Snapchat, and the smartphone camera manufacturers captured this market. Kodak didn't see it.

#### The Enduring Lesson

Kodak's failure is not a story of complacency or stupidity. It is a story of the structural impossibility of disrupting yourself when your entire organization — its people, its cost structure, its culture, its investor base — is optimized for the existing business. The innovation was identified. The threat was understood by many inside the company. But the organization could not respond in time.

This is the Innovator's Dilemma in its purest form. And it is happening right now in automotive (ICE vs. EV), banking (traditional vs. fintech), healthcare (fee-for-service vs. value-based care), and media (linear TV vs. streaming).
